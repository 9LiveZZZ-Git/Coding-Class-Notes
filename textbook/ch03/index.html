3.1 Sequences and Series

Before we can understand fractals, we need the mathematical language of sequences and series. These concepts tell us how repeated processes behave over time — whether they settle down, blow up, or oscillate forever. While these may seem like abstract mathematical concepts, they are the precise tools we need to answer the question at the heart of the Mandelbrot set: "does this sequence stay bounded?"

Definition

Sequence — An ordered list of numbers $a_1, a_2, a_3, \ldots$ generated by some rule. Each number $a_n$ is called a term, and $n$ is its index.

Arithmetic Sequences

An arithmetic sequence has a constant difference $d$ between consecutive terms:

$$a_n = a_1 + (n-1)d$$

For example, $2, 5, 8, 11, 14, \ldots$ is arithmetic with $a_1 = 2$ and $d = 3$. The partial sum of the first $n$ terms of an arithmetic sequence is:

$$S_n = \frac{n}{2}(a_1 + a_n) = \frac{n}{2}\bigl(2a_1 + (n-1)d\bigr)$$

The famous story of young Carl Friedrich Gauss summing $1 + 2 + 3 + \cdots + 100$ uses this formula: $S_{100} = \frac{100}{2}(1 + 100) = 5050$.

Geometric Sequences

A geometric sequence has a constant ratio $r$ between consecutive terms:

$$a_n = a_1 \cdot r^{\,n-1}$$

For example, $1, \tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{4}, \tfrac{1}{8}, \ldots$ is geometric with $a_1 = 1$ and $r = \tfrac{1}{2}$. The partial sum is:

$$S_n = a_1 \cdot \frac{1 - r^n}{1 - r} \quad (r \neq 1)$$

When $|r| < 1$, the infinite sum converges:

$$S_\infty = \frac{a_1}{1 - r}$$

So $1 + \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{4} + \tfrac{1}{8} + \cdots = \frac{1}{1 - 1/2} = 2$. This is the mathematical foundation for understanding which iterations stay bounded and which escape to infinity.

Key Concept

The Mandelbrot set is fundamentally about classifying whether a sequence converges or diverges. For each point in the complex plane, we generate a sequence through iteration and ask: does it stay bounded, or does it escape to infinity?

Series and Partial Sums

A series is the sum of the terms of a sequence. We distinguish between:

  • Finite series (partial sums): $S_n = \displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} a_k$
  • Infinite series: $S = \displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} a_k = \lim_{n \to \infty} S_n$

The infinite series exists (converges) only if the sequence of partial sums approaches a finite limit.

The Harmonic Series: A Subtle Trap

The harmonic series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \tfrac{1}{n} = 1 + \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{3} + \tfrac{1}{4} + \cdots$ is one of the most important examples in mathematics. Even though each term shrinks toward zero, the sum grows without bound. The proof (by Nicole Oresme, c. 1350) groups terms cleverly:

$$1 + \frac{1}{2} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{4}}_{> 1/2} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{5} + \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{7} + \frac{1}{8}}_{> 1/2} + \cdots$$

Each group of $2^k$ terms sums to more than $\tfrac{1}{2}$, so the total exceeds $\tfrac{1}{2} \cdot k$ for any $k$ — it grows without limit. However, it grows extraordinarily slowly: you need about $e^{10} \approx 22{,}000$ terms for the sum to exceed $10$, and about $e^{100} \approx 10^{43}$ terms to exceed $100$.

This matters for fractals because it teaches us that convergence of terms does not guarantee convergence of sums. We need to be careful about what "bounded" means.

Zeno's Paradox and Geometric Series

The ancient Greek philosopher Zeno of Elea (c. 490–430 BCE) posed a famous paradox: to walk across a room, you must first cross half the distance, then half the remaining distance, then half again, and so on. This requires completing infinitely many tasks — which seems impossible.

The resolution is the convergence of the geometric series:

$$\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{8} + \frac{1}{16} + \cdots = \frac{1/2}{1 - 1/2} = 1$$

The infinitely many steps take a finite total distance (and time). This is precisely the kind of thinking we need for the Mandelbrot set: we ask whether infinitely many iterations produce a bounded result, and the mathematics of series gives us the framework to answer.

Power Series and Radius of Convergence

A power series $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n$ converges for some values of $x$ and diverges for others. The radius of convergence $R$ defines the boundary: the series converges absolutely for $|x| < R$ and diverges for $|x| > R$.

This concept directly parallels the Mandelbrot set: for the iteration $z^2 + c$, we are asking whether the "orbit" of $z$ stays within a radius of convergence (the escape radius $2$). Points inside the set are like values inside the radius of convergence; points outside are like values where the series diverges.

Telescoping Series

A telescoping series is one where most terms cancel when the partial sum is expanded. For example:

$$\sum_{n=1}^{N} \frac{1}{n(n+1)} = \sum_{n=1}^{N} \left(\frac{1}{n} - \frac{1}{n+1}\right) = 1 - \frac{1}{N+1} \to 1$$

The intermediate terms cancel like the sections of a collapsing telescope, leaving only the first and last terms. This elegant technique is used in proofs about convergence and in some fractal coloring algorithms where we need to compute sums efficiently.

Connecting to Computation

In a computer program, we can never compute an infinite series exactly. We always truncate at some finite $N$ (the maxIter parameter in our Mandelbrot code). The key question is: is $N$ large enough that our approximation is good?

For the Mandelbrot set, we are not computing a sum but classifying behavior — does the orbit escape or stay bounded? We use two heuristics:

  1. Early termination: If $|z_n| > 2$, the orbit will definitely diverge. We stop immediately.
  2. Maximum iterations: If $|z_n| \leq 2$ after maxIter steps, we assume the orbit is bounded. Higher maxIter values give more accurate boundaries but cost more computation.

Near the boundary of the Mandelbrot set, orbits can remain bounded for thousands of iterations before finally escaping. This is why zooming into the boundary requires increasing maxIter proportionally — the fractal’s infinite detail demands infinite patience (or infinite iterations) to resolve perfectly.

Common Mistake

Using too few iterations (maxIter) is the most common source of poor Mandelbrot renders. With maxIter = 20, the boundary looks smooth and boring. With maxIter = 100, fine detail appears. With maxIter = 1000, extraordinary filaments and spirals emerge. When zooming in, always increase maxIter to match the zoom level.

3.2 Convergence and Divergence

Definition

Convergence — A sequence $\{a_n\}$ converges to a limit $L$ if, for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists an integer $N$ such that $|a_n - L| < \varepsilon$ for all $n > N$. We write $\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = L$.

In plain language: no matter how tiny a target band you draw around $L$, the sequence eventually enters that band and never leaves. Think of it like a dartboard with an infinitely shrinkable bullseye — no matter how small you make the bullseye, all darts thrown after some point $N$ will land inside it.

The Epsilon-N Definition Visualized

Consider the sequence $a_n = 1/n$. We claim it converges to $L = 0$. To verify:

  • Choose $\varepsilon = 0.1$. Then $|a_n - 0| = 1/n < 0.1$ whenever $n > 10$. So $N = 10$.
  • Choose $\varepsilon = 0.01$. Then $1/n < 0.01$ whenever $n > 100$. So $N = 100$.
  • Choose $\varepsilon = 0.001$. Then $N = 1000$.

No matter how small we make $\varepsilon$, we can always find a large enough $N$. This is exactly what the definition requires.

Definition

Divergence — A sequence diverges if it does not converge. The terms may grow without bound ($a_n \to \pm\infty$) or fail to settle on any single value (oscillation).

Examples

  • Converges: $1, \tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{4}, \tfrac{1}{8}, \ldots \to 0$. Each term is half the previous.
  • Diverges (growth): $1, 2, 4, 8, 16, \ldots \to \infty$. Exponential growth.
  • Diverges (oscillation): $1, -1, 1, -1, \ldots$ bounces forever without settling.
  • Subtle divergence: The harmonic series $\sum 1/n$ diverges even though each term approaches $0$. The partial sums grow without bound, just very slowly.

Tests for Convergence

Theorem: Geometric Series Test

The geometric series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} ar^n$ converges to $\dfrac{a}{1-r}$ if $|r| < 1$, and diverges if $|r| \geq 1$.

Theorem: Divergence Test (Necessary Condition)

If $\displaystyle\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n \neq 0$, then $\sum a_n$ diverges. (Warning: the converse is not true — $\lim a_n = 0$ does not guarantee convergence, as the harmonic series shows.)

Ratio Test: For a series $\sum a_n$, if $\lim_{n \to \infty} |a_{n+1}/a_n| = L$, then the series converges if $L < 1$ and diverges if $L > 1$.

Comparison Test: If $0 \leq a_n \leq b_n$ for all $n$ and $\sum b_n$ converges, then $\sum a_n$ converges. Conversely, if $\sum a_n$ diverges, then $\sum b_n$ diverges.

For our fractal purposes, the key test is simple: if the magnitudes of our iterated values stay below some bound (we use $|z| \leq 2$ for the Mandelbrot set), the sequence is bounded and potentially convergent. If $|z|$ exceeds $2$, it will diverge to infinity.

3.3 Iteration and Feedback

Definition

Iteration — The repetition of a process where the output of each step becomes the input to the next. Given a function $f$ and a starting value $x_0$, we define the recurrence relation: $$x_{n+1} = f(x_n)$$

Iteration is the engine of chaos and fractals. A simple rule, applied repeatedly, can produce behavior ranging from calm convergence to wild, unpredictable chaos. In computing, iteration is ubiquitous — every loop, every recursive algorithm, every simulation timestep is an instance of iteration.

"...the repetition of a process in order to generate a (possibly unbounded) sequence of outcomes. Each repetition of the process is a single iteration, and the outcome of each iteration is then the starting point of the next iteration." — Wikipedia

Feedback

Feedback occurs when the output of a system is routed back as input, forming a loop. Audio feedback — a microphone picking up its own speaker output — is a familiar example. The screech you hear results from a signal being amplified, output, captured, and amplified again: iteration with feedback.

In code, a for loop with the assignment x = f(x) is exactly this pattern: the output of f becomes the next input.

Fixed Points

Definition

Fixed Point — A value $x^*$ where $f(x^*) = x^*$. Applying the function does not change the value. A fixed point is attracting (stable) if nearby iterations converge toward it, and repelling (unstable) if they move away.

The stability criterion is elegant: if $|f'(x^*)| < 1$, the fixed point is attracting. If $|f'(x^*)| > 1$, it is repelling. When $|f'(x^*)| = 1$, the behavior is marginal and requires more careful analysis.

Finding Fixed Points

To find the fixed points of a function $f$, we solve $f(x) = x$, or equivalently $f(x) - x = 0$. For example:

  • $f(x) = x^2$: Solve $x^2 = x$, giving $x(x-1) = 0$, so $x^* = 0$ or $x^* = 1$. Since $f'(x) = 2x$, we have $|f'(0)| = 0 < 1$ (super-attracting) and $|f'(1)| = 2 > 1$ (repelling).
  • $f(x) = 2x(1-x)$: (logistic map with $r=2$) Solve $2x(1-x) = x$, giving $2x - 2x^2 = x$, so $x(1 - 2x) = 0$. Fixed points: $x^* = 0$ and $x^* = 1/2$. Since $f'(x) = 2 - 4x$, we get $|f'(0)| = 2 > 1$ (repelling) and $|f'(1/2)| = 0$ (super-attracting).
  • $f(x) = \cos(x)$: Solve $\cos(x) = x$ numerically: $x^* \approx 0.7390851332$. Since $|f'(x^*)| = |-\sin(0.739)| \approx 0.674 < 1$, the fixed point is attracting.

Examples:

  • $\cos(0.7390851\ldots) = 0.7390851\ldots$ — attracting fixed point of cosine. Since $|\!-\!\sin(0.739)| \approx 0.674 < 1$, convergence is quick.
  • $\sin(0) = 0$ — attracting fixed point of sine. Since $|\cos(0)| = 1$, convergence is extremely slow (marginal stability).
  • $2 \cdot 0 = 0$ — fixed point of doubling, but repelling since $|f'(0)| = 2 > 1$. Any tiny perturbation gets amplified.

Periodic Orbits and $n$-Cycles

Not every iteration converges to a fixed point. Sometimes the values cycle through a repeating pattern:

Definition

$n$-Cycle (Periodic Orbit) — A sequence of $n$ distinct values $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n$ such that $f(x_1) = x_2, f(x_2) = x_3, \ldots, f(x_n) = x_1$. Equivalently, $f^n(x_1) = x_1$ where $f^n$ denotes applying $f$ a total of $n$ times. A 2-cycle alternates between two values; a 3-cycle visits three values before repeating.

For the logistic map $f(x) = rx(1-x)$, increasing $r$ past $3.0$ causes the stable fixed point to lose stability and give birth to a stable 2-cycle. The orbit bounces back and forth between two values. As $r$ increases further, this 2-cycle itself bifurcates into a 4-cycle, then an 8-cycle, and so on — the period-doubling cascade we explore in the next section.

A remarkable theorem due to Li and Yorke (1975) states: "Period three implies chaos." If a one-dimensional map has a 3-cycle, then it must also have periodic orbits of every period $n$, and must exhibit chaotic behavior for some starting points. This theorem, whose title became one of the most famous phrases in dynamical systems theory, shows that even the existence of a single period-3 orbit guarantees the full complexity of chaos.

Convergence Speed

Not all convergent iterations converge at the same rate. The speed depends on the derivative of $f$ at the fixed point:

  • Fast convergence ($|f'(x^*)| \ll 1$): $x_{n+1} = \cos(x_n)$ converges to $0.7390851\ldots$ within about 50 iterations. Since $|f'(x^*)| = |\!-\!\sin(0.739)| \approx 0.674$, each error is multiplied by roughly $0.674$ per step.
  • Slow convergence ($|f'(x^*)| \approx 1$): $x_{n+1} = \sin(x_n)$ converges to $0$, but $|f'(0)| = |\cos(0)| = 1$ (marginal). Even after 1000 iterations, $x$ has only decreased to about $0.044$. The convergence rate is sub-linear.
  • Divergence ($|f'(x^*)| > 1$): $x_{n+1} = 2x_n$ has a fixed point at $0$ with $|f'(0)| = 2$. Any perturbation doubles each step — exponential divergence.
Theorem: Linear Convergence

If $|f'(x^*)| = \rho < 1$ at a fixed point $x^*$, then the iteration $x_{n+1} = f(x_n)$ converges linearly with rate $\rho$. That is, the error satisfies $|x_{n+1} - x^*| \approx \rho \cdot |x_n - x^*|$ for $x_n$ near $x^*$. The smaller $\rho$ is, the faster the convergence.

Cobweb Plots

A cobweb plot (also called a staircase diagram) is a graphical tool for visualizing iteration. On the same axes we plot both the function $y = f(x)$ and the identity line $y = x$. Starting from $x_0$:

  1. Go vertically to the curve $y = f(x)$: this gives $f(x_0)$.
  2. Go horizontally to the line $y = x$: this transfers the new value back to the x-axis.
  3. Repeat.

If the cobweb spirals inward toward the intersection of the curve and the line, the iteration converges to that fixed point. If it spirals outward, the iteration diverges.

3.4 The Logistic Map

The logistic map is the simplest mathematical model that produces genuinely chaotic behavior. It is defined by:

$$x_{n+1} = r \, x_n \,(1 - x_n)$$

where $x_n \in [0, 1]$ represents a population (as a fraction of some carrying capacity) and $r \in [0, 4]$ is a growth-rate parameter. Despite its simplicity — just one variable, one parameter, and one line of algebra — the logistic map exhibits the full spectrum of dynamical behavior: fixed points, periodic orbits, period-doubling cascades, and genuine chaos.

The logistic map can be understood ecologically: the term $rx_n$ represents exponential growth (reproduction), while the factor $(1 - x_n)$ represents density-dependent limitation (competition for resources). When the population is small ($x_n \approx 0$), growth is nearly exponential. When it approaches the carrying capacity ($x_n \approx 1$), growth slows and may reverse.

Fixed Points of the Logistic Map

To find the fixed points, we solve $rx(1-x) = x$:

$$rx - rx^2 = x \implies x(r - rx - 1) = 0 \implies x(r - 1 - rx) = 0$$

The solutions are:

  • $x^*_1 = 0$ (trivial fixed point, always exists)
  • $x^*_2 = 1 - 1/r$ (nontrivial fixed point, exists for $r > 1$)

The stability of the nontrivial fixed point is determined by $f'(x^*_2) = r(1 - 2x^*_2) = r \cdot (1 - 2 + 2/r) = 2 - r$. So the fixed point is stable when $|2 - r| < 1$, i.e., when $1 < r < 3$. At $r = 3$, the fixed point loses stability and the first period doubling occurs.

Historical Note

Biologist Robert May popularized the logistic map in a landmark 1976 Nature paper. He showed that even the simplest ecological models can produce extraordinarily complex dynamics, arguing that "not only in research, but also in the everyday world of politics and economics, we would all be better off if more people realized that simple nonlinear systems do not necessarily possess simple dynamical properties."

Behavior as $r$ Changes

The logistic map shows a dramatic transition from order to chaos as $r$ increases:

  • $0 < r < 1$: The population dies out. The sequence converges to $x = 0$.
  • $1 < r < 3$: The population settles to a stable equilibrium $x^* = 1 - 1/r$.
  • $3 < r < 3.449\ldots$: The fixed point becomes unstable. The sequence oscillates between two values (period-2 cycle).
  • $3.449 < r < 3.544\ldots$: Period doubling: the sequence oscillates between four values.
  • $r \approx 3.5699\ldots$: After infinitely many period doublings, the system becomes chaotic. The sequence never repeats.
  • $r > 3.5699\ldots$: Mostly chaotic, but with windows of periodicity (e.g., a period-3 window near $r = 3.83$).

The Feigenbaum Constant

Theorem: Feigenbaum Universality

Let $r_n$ be the parameter value at which the $n$-th period doubling occurs. The ratio of successive bifurcation intervals converges to the Feigenbaum constant: $$\delta = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{r_n - r_{n-1}}{r_{n+1} - r_n} = 4.669201609\ldots$$ This constant is universal: it appears in every one-dimensional map undergoing period doubling, regardless of the specific function. It is a fundamental constant of chaos theory, analogous to $\pi$ in geometry or $e$ in analysis.

Windows of Order in Chaos

Even in the chaotic regime ($r > 3.5699$), there are narrow "windows" where periodic behavior reappears. The most prominent is the period-3 window near $r = 3.83$. The mathematicians Li and Yorke proved a famous theorem in their 1975 paper titled simply "Period Three Implies Chaos": if a continuous map of the interval has a periodic orbit of period 3, it must also have periodic orbits of every other period, plus uncountably many non-periodic (chaotic) orbits. This paper gave the field its name.

The self-similar structure of the bifurcation diagram is itself fractal. If you zoom into any of the period-doubling cascades within the chaotic windows, you see a miniature copy of the entire diagram — the same self-similarity that characterizes the Mandelbrot set.

Sensitivity to Initial Conditions in the Logistic Map

At $r = 4$ (fully chaotic regime), the logistic map is equivalent to the Bernoulli shift, one of the simplest possible chaotic systems. Two orbits starting at $x_0 = 0.5000000$ and $x_0 = 0.5000001$ will diverge completely within about 50 iterations. This is sensitive dependence in action: a difference of one part in ten million grows to span the entire range $[0, 1]$.

This has profound practical implications: no finite-precision computer can predict the long-term behavior of the logistic map at $r = 4$. After enough iterations, rounding errors dominate and the computed orbit bears no relation to the "true" orbit. This is not a bug in the software — it is a fundamental property of the mathematics.

The Logistic Map as Artistic Metaphor

For artists and creative coders, the logistic map offers profound conceptual lessons:

  • Simple rules create complex output. The one-line formula $rx(1-x)$ produces the full spectrum from boring equilibrium to visual chaos. In generative art, the simplest algorithms often yield the richest results.
  • Parameters are everything. The same rule with different values of $r$ produces completely different behavior. In creative coding, the "interesting" parameter ranges are often narrow and must be discovered through exploration.
  • Order and chaos coexist. Within the chaotic regime, windows of periodicity appear. This interweaving of order and disorder is aesthetically compelling and mirrors patterns in music, visual art, and nature.
  • Control is an illusion. Near the boundary between order and chaos, tiny parameter changes produce dramatically different outcomes. This teaches humility about prediction and control in complex systems.

3.5 Chaos Theory

Chaos theory studies deterministic systems whose behavior is, for practical purposes, unpredictable. The system follows exact rules with zero randomness, yet tiny differences in starting conditions lead to completely different outcomes.

Historical Note

Meteorologist Edward Lorenz discovered chaos accidentally in 1961. Restarting a weather simulation from rounded intermediate values (three decimal places instead of six) produced completely different weather patterns. The difference — about one part in a thousand — grew exponentially. James Gleick's 1987 bestseller Chaos: Making a New Science brought these ideas to a broad audience and remains the definitive popular introduction.

The Butterfly Effect

The butterfly effect — the idea that a butterfly flapping its wings in Brazil could set off a tornado in Texas — is the popular name for sensitive dependence on initial conditions:

Definition

Sensitive Dependence on Initial Conditions — A dynamical system exhibits sensitive dependence if there exists a $\delta > 0$ such that, for any point $x$ and any neighborhood of $x$ (no matter how small), there is a nearby point $y$ whose trajectory eventually diverges from that of $x$ by at least $\delta$. Formally: for all $\varepsilon > 0$ there exist $y$ with $|x - y| < \varepsilon$ and $n$ with $|f^n(x) - f^n(y)| > \delta$.

This is why long-range weather forecasting is fundamentally limited: we can never measure initial atmospheric conditions with infinite precision, and the atmosphere amplifies these tiny measurement errors exponentially. Current weather models can predict about 10–14 days ahead with reasonable accuracy; beyond that, the butterfly effect overwhelms the forecast.

Lorenz articulated this memorably in a 1972 talk titled "Predictability: Does the Flap of a Butterfly's Wings in Brazil Set Off a Tornado in Texas?" The answer is both yes and no: a single butterfly cannot meaningfully be said to "cause" a specific tornado, but the extreme sensitivity means that the presence or absence of the butterfly does change the detailed evolution of the atmosphere. This is the paradox of deterministic chaos: the system is fully determined by its initial conditions, but those conditions are never fully known.

Nonlinearity

Chaos requires nonlinearity. A linear system satisfies $f(ax + by) = af(x) + bf(y)$: the output is proportional to the input, and small changes produce proportionally small effects.

Nonlinear operations include squaring ($x^2$), multiplication of variables ($xy$), and trigonometric functions ($\sin x$, $\cos x$). These can amplify small differences, creating sensitive dependence. The Mandelbrot recurrence $z^2 + c$ is nonlinear because of the squaring — this nonlinearity creates the fractal boundary.

Strange Attractors

A strange attractor is the geometric shape traced by a chaotic system's trajectories in state space. Unlike a fixed point (a dot) or a limit cycle (a loop), a strange attractor has fractal structure with detail at every scale. The most famous is the Lorenz attractor, shaped like a butterfly, arising from Lorenz's simplified weather equations:

$$\frac{dx}{dt} = \sigma(y - x), \qquad \frac{dy}{dt} = x(\rho - z) - y, \qquad \frac{dz}{dt} = xy - \beta z$$

With the classic parameters $\sigma = 10$, $\rho = 28$, $\beta = 8/3$, the system never settles into a periodic orbit but traces out the characteristic butterfly shape forever. The trajectory switches unpredictably between the two "wings" of the attractor — the same sensitive dependence that makes weather unpredictable.

Fractals: The Geometry of Chaos

Fractals are the geometric fingerprints of chaotic dynamical systems. Benoit Mandelbrot coined the term in 1975, from the Latin fractus meaning "broken" or "fragmented." He defined a fractal loosely as a shape whose Hausdorff dimension exceeds its topological dimension — in other words, a shape that is "rougher" than any smooth curve or surface.

Key properties of fractals:

  • Self-similarity: Parts of the shape resemble the whole, at every scale. Zooming into the Mandelbrot set boundary reveals miniature copies of the entire set.
  • Infinite detail: No matter how far you zoom in, new structure appears. There is no "smooth" level at which the complexity stops.
  • Fractional dimension: The Mandelbrot set boundary has a Hausdorff dimension of 2 (it is a curve that is so convoluted it fills an area), while the Koch snowflake has dimension $\log 4 / \log 3 \approx 1.2619$.
  • Simple generating rules: Despite infinite complexity, fractals are generated by simple iterative processes. The Mandelbrot set comes from $z^2 + c$ — just four characters of algebra.
Historical Note

The connection between chaos and fractals runs deep. When you plot the boundary between convergence and divergence for any nonlinear iterative system, that boundary is typically a fractal. This is true for the Mandelbrot set, for Newton fractals, for the logistic map's bifurcation diagram, and for the basins of attraction of any chaotic system. Chaos creates fractals; fractals are the geometry of chaos.

Lyapunov Exponents

The Lyapunov exponent $\lambda$ quantifies the rate of trajectory divergence. If two trajectories start a distance $\delta_0$ apart, after $n$ iterations they are approximately $\delta_0 \cdot e^{\lambda n}$ apart:

  • $\lambda < 0$: trajectories converge (stable, non-chaotic)
  • $\lambda = 0$: marginal (boundary between order and chaos)
  • $\lambda > 0$: trajectories diverge exponentially (chaos)

A positive Lyapunov exponent is the mathematical signature of chaos. For the logistic map at $r = 4$, the Lyapunov exponent is $\lambda = \ln 2 \approx 0.693$, meaning nearby orbits diverge at a rate of roughly $2^n$ after $n$ iterations.

The Three Ingredients of Chaos

A dynamical system is considered chaotic if it has all three of these properties:

  1. Sensitive dependence on initial conditions: Nearby trajectories diverge exponentially ($\lambda > 0$).
  2. Topological transitivity: The system cannot be decomposed into two invariant open sets. Informally, any region of state space eventually visits every other region.
  3. Dense periodic orbits: Periodic orbits are dense in the state space — every point is arbitrarily close to some periodic orbit.

These three conditions, formalized by Robert Devaney, capture the essence of chaos: unpredictability (1), indecomposability (2), and an element of regularity (3) that prevents the system from being "just random."

Chaos vs. Randomness

A crucial distinction: chaotic systems are deterministic, not random. Given exactly the same initial conditions, a chaotic system will always produce exactly the same output. The unpredictability arises only because we can never measure or specify initial conditions with infinite precision.

This is different from true randomness (as in quantum mechanics), where identical preparations can produce genuinely different outcomes. Chaos is deterministic unpredictability; randomness is fundamental indeterminacy.

This distinction has practical consequences for art and simulation. A chaotic system can be replayed exactly by preserving the initial conditions — this is how generative art pieces can be "seeded" with a number and reproduced identically. True random processes cannot be reproduced without recording every step.

Examples of Chaos in Nature and Art

Chaotic dynamics appear throughout nature, science, and creative practice:

  • Weather and climate: The atmosphere is the canonical chaotic system. Weather prediction beyond about two weeks is fundamentally impossible due to sensitive dependence.
  • Turbulent fluid flow: The transition from smooth (laminar) flow to turbulence is a route to chaos. Smoke rising from a candle starts smooth and becomes chaotic as it rises.
  • Population dynamics: Ecological populations can exhibit chaotic oscillations, as modeled by the logistic map. Insect populations, in particular, have been observed to follow period-doubling routes to chaos.
  • The double pendulum: A pendulum attached to the end of another pendulum is one of the simplest physical systems that exhibits chaos. Two double pendulums released from nearly identical positions will quickly diverge into wildly different trajectories.
  • Heart rhythms: The healthy heart exhibits a form of chaotic variability. Paradoxically, perfectly regular heartbeats can be a sign of pathology. Some researchers argue that the loss of chaos in cardiac dynamics precedes heart failure.
  • Dripping faucets: At certain flow rates, water drips follow a chaotic pattern that has been experimentally observed to undergo period-doubling cascades similar to the logistic map.
  • Electronic circuits: Simple circuits containing nonlinear elements (like Chua's circuit) can produce chaotic oscillations, visualized as strange attractors on an oscilloscope.

Chaos and Artistic Practice

For computational artists, chaos offers a paradox of control: the system is fully deterministic (repeatable), yet its behavior is complex enough to surprise the creator. This tension between authorship and emergence is central to generative art.

Techniques for harnessing chaos in art include:

  • Parameter exploration: Slowly varying a control parameter (like $r$ in the logistic map) to move between ordered and chaotic regimes, creating visual transitions from regularity to complexity.
  • Strange attractor visualization: Plotting the trajectories of chaotic systems as particle trails, often with transparency, to reveal the fractal structure of the attractor.
  • Sensitive dependence as texture: Using the divergence rate (Lyapunov exponent) as a coloring parameter creates textures that highlight the boundary between order and chaos.
  • Audio-driven chaos: Using audio input to modulate the parameters of a chaotic system, creating visuals that respond to sound in complex, non-obvious ways.
Key Concept

Fractals are the geometric fingerprints of chaotic systems. When you map out which initial conditions converge and which diverge, the boundary between these regions is a fractal — a shape with infinite detail and self-similarity at every scale.

3.6 Complex Numbers

To define the Mandelbrot set, we need numbers that live in two dimensions. Real numbers are one-dimensional (they live on a number line). Complex numbers are two-dimensional — they live on a plane.

Definition

Complex Number — A number of the form $z = a + bi$, where $a$ is the real part (written $\text{Re}(z)$), $b$ is the imaginary part ($\text{Im}(z)$), and $i$ is the imaginary unit defined by $i^2 = -1$.

Why "Imaginary"?

The name "imaginary" is unfortunate and historically misleading. It was coined by Descartes in 1637 as a pejorative — he considered $\sqrt{-1}$ to be a nonsensical quantity. But complex numbers are no less "real" than real numbers; they are simply a different kind of mathematical object, one that happens to be two-dimensional rather than one-dimensional.

Gauss preferred the term "lateral numbers" (reflecting their perpendicular direction on the number plane), and many mathematicians wish that name had stuck. In any case, $i$ is a perfectly well-defined mathematical entity: it is the number whose square is $-1$, and the algebra that follows from this definition is consistent, powerful, and beautiful.

Historical Note

Complex numbers were first used seriously by Gerolamo Cardano in 1545 to solve cubic equations, and the full geometric interpretation (the complex plane) was developed independently by Caspar Wessel (1799), Jean-Robert Argand (1806), and Carl Friedrich Gauss (1831). Gauss used complex numbers extensively in his proof of the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra: every polynomial of degree $n$ has exactly $n$ roots in $\mathbb{C}$.

The Complex Plane

Every complex number $z = a + bi$ corresponds to a point $(a, b)$ on the complex plane (also called the Argand diagram). The horizontal axis is the real axis; the vertical axis is the imaginary axis. This geometric interpretation transforms abstract algebra into visual geometry.

The complex plane includes the real numbers as a special case (the horizontal axis, where $b = 0$) and the pure imaginary numbers (the vertical axis, where $a = 0$). Every other complex number has both a real and an imaginary component.

Important landmarks on the complex plane:

  • Origin $0 + 0i = 0$: the zero of the complex plane
  • Unity $1 + 0i = 1$: one unit along the real axis
  • Imaginary unit $0 + 1i = i$: one unit along the imaginary axis
  • Unit circle: all points $z$ with $|z| = 1$, parameterized by $e^{i\theta}$ for $\theta \in [0, 2\pi)$

The unit circle is especially important: complex multiplication by a point on the unit circle is a pure rotation (no scaling). This is why the angle (argument) of a complex number determines its rotational effect, while the magnitude determines its scaling effect.

Polar Form and Euler's Formula

A complex number can also be expressed in polar form:

$$z = r \cdot e^{i\theta} = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$$

where:

  • $r = |z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$ is the magnitude (or modulus) — the distance from the origin
  • $\theta = \arg(z) = \text{atan2}(b, a)$ is the argument (or phase) — the angle from the positive real axis

This is Euler's formula, one of the most beautiful identities in all of mathematics. It says that exponentiating an imaginary number produces a point on the unit circle at angle $\theta$.

Theorem: Euler's Identity

The special case $\theta = \pi$ gives the famous identity: $$e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$$ This single equation connects five fundamental mathematical constants — $e$ (the base of natural logarithms), $i$ (the imaginary unit), $\pi$ (the ratio of circumference to diameter), $1$ (the multiplicative identity), and $0$ (the additive identity) — in a relationship of breathtaking economy. Richard Feynman called it "the most remarkable formula in mathematics."

The conversions between Cartesian and polar forms are:

$$r = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}, \quad \theta = \text{atan2}(b,\, a), \quad a = r\cos\theta, \quad b = r\sin\theta$$

Operations on Complex Numbers

Addition — add real and imaginary parts separately:

$$(a + bi) + (c + di) = (a + c) + (b + d)i$$

Multiplication — expand using $i^2 = -1$:

$$(a + bi)(c + di) = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i$$

In polar form, multiplication is elegant: magnitudes multiply and angles add:

$$r_1 e^{i\theta_1} \cdot r_2 e^{i\theta_2} = r_1 r_2 \cdot e^{i(\theta_1 + \theta_2)}$$

This means multiplying by a complex number simultaneously scales (by the magnitude) and rotates (by the argument). This geometric interpretation is profound:

  • Multiplying by $i$ rotates 90 degrees counterclockwise (and $i \cdot i = -1$ rotates 180 degrees, which is negation).
  • Multiplying by $e^{i\pi/4} = \frac{1+i}{\sqrt{2}}$ rotates 45 degrees.
  • Multiplying by $2e^{i\pi/6}$ scales by 2 and rotates 30 degrees.

For the Mandelbrot iteration, squaring doubles the angle and squares the magnitude: $(re^{i\theta})^2 = r^2 e^{2i\theta}$. This is why points with $|z| > 1$ spiral outward (magnitude grows: $r^2 > r$) and points with $|z| < 1$ spiral inward (magnitude shrinks: $r^2 < r$) under repeated squaring. Points on the unit circle ($|z| = 1$) stay on the unit circle under squaring but rotate at double speed.

Adding $c$ perturbs this pure rotational/scaling behavior. The interplay between the squaring (which wants to amplify differences and rotate) and the constant $c$ (which shifts the center) creates the Mandelbrot set's boundary. Different values of $c$ tip the balance differently, which is why the set has such an intricate shape.

The Complex Conjugate

The conjugate of $z = a + bi$ is $\bar{z} = a - bi$ — the reflection across the real axis. Important properties:

  • $z \cdot \bar{z} = a^2 + b^2 = |z|^2$ (always a non-negative real number)
  • $\overline{z_1 + z_2} = \bar{z_1} + \bar{z_2}$ and $\overline{z_1 z_2} = \bar{z_1}\bar{z_2}$
  • $z + \bar{z} = 2a$ (twice the real part), $z - \bar{z} = 2bi$ (twice the imaginary part)

The conjugate is used in complex division: $\frac{1}{z} = \frac{\bar{z}}{|z|^2}$. It also explains the Mandelbrot set's symmetry: since the iteration preserves conjugation ($\overline{z^2 + c} = \bar{z}^2 + \bar{c}$), the set is symmetric about the real axis.

Squaring (the key operation for the Mandelbrot set):

$$(a + bi)^2 = (a^2 - b^2) + (2ab)i$$

In polar form: squaring doubles the angle and squares the magnitude.

Magnitude: $|z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$ — the distance from the origin to the point $(a, b)$.

Argument: $\arg(z) = \text{atan2}(b, a)$ — the angle the point makes with the positive real axis.

Common Mistake

When computing $\arg(z)$, always use atan2(b, a) rather than atan(b/a). The two-argument form correctly handles all four quadrants, while atan(b/a) cannot distinguish between $(1, 1)$ and $(-1, -1)$.

Worked Example: Squaring and the Mandelbrot Iteration

Let us trace one step of the Mandelbrot iteration by hand. Suppose $c = 0.3 + 0.5i$ and the current value is $z = 0.1 + 0.7i$. We need to compute $z^2 + c$:

Step 1: Compute $z^2$.

$$z^2 = (0.1 + 0.7i)^2 = (0.1^2 - 0.7^2) + (2 \cdot 0.1 \cdot 0.7)i = (0.01 - 0.49) + 0.14i = -0.48 + 0.14i$$

Step 2: Add $c$.

$$z^2 + c = (-0.48 + 0.14i) + (0.3 + 0.5i) = -0.18 + 0.64i$$

Step 3: Check escape.

$$|z_{\text{new}}| = \sqrt{(-0.18)^2 + (0.64)^2} = \sqrt{0.0324 + 0.4096} = \sqrt{0.4420} \approx 0.665$$

Since $0.665 < 2$, the point has not escaped. We continue iterating.

Complex Numbers in GLSL

GLSL has no complex number type, so we represent them as vec2:

// Argument: atan() with two arguments

This representation is efficient and natural for GPU computation. The key thing to remember is that while addition, subtraction, magnitude, and argument all work with built-in functions, multiplication must be implemented manually because vec2 * vec2 does component-wise multiplication, not complex multiplication.

3.7 The Mandelbrot Set

The Mandelbrot set is the most famous object in fractal mathematics. It combines iteration, complex numbers, and the convergence/divergence classification into a single, infinitely complex shape.

Definition

The Mandelbrot Set $\mathcal{M}$ is the set of all complex numbers $c$ for which the iteration $$z_{n+1} = z_n^2 + c, \qquad z_0 = 0$$ does not diverge to infinity. Equivalently, $c \in \mathcal{M}$ if and only if $\sup_n |z_n| < \infty$.

The Algorithm

For each point $c$ in the complex plane:

  1. Start with $z_0 = 0$.
  2. Iterate: $z_1 = c$, then $z_2 = c^2 + c$, then $z_3 = (c^2 + c)^2 + c$, and so on.
  3. If $|z_n| > 2$ for any $n$, then $c$ is not in the Mandelbrot set.
  4. If $|z_n| \leq 2$ for all $n$ up to some maximum iteration count, then $c$ is approximately in the set.
Theorem: Escape Radius

If $|z_n| > \max(|c|, 2)$ at any iteration, the sequence $\{z_n\}$ diverges to infinity. Therefore $2$ is a sufficient escape radius: once $|z_n| > 2$, the point is guaranteed to escape. (This follows from the triangle inequality applied to $z_{n+1} = z_n^2 + c$.)

Anatomy of the Mandelbrot Set

The Mandelbrot set has rich internal structure:

  • Main cardioid: The large heart-shaped region centered near $(-0.25, 0)$. Points here converge to a single fixed point. Its boundary is the cardioid $r = \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{1}{2}\cos\theta$.
  • Period-2 bulb: The circular bulb to the left, centered at $(-1, 0)$ with radius $\tfrac{1}{4}$. Iterations here oscillate between two values.
  • Period-$n$ bulbs: Smaller bulbs attached to the cardioid and to each other. Each produces orbits of period $n$.
  • Fractal boundary: The boundary has infinite detail and self-similarity at every scale.

Connection to Julia Sets

Every point $c$ has an associated Julia set (Section 3.9). The Mandelbrot set acts as a "map" of all Julia sets:

  • If $c \in \mathcal{M}$ (inside), the Julia set $J_c$ is connected (one piece).
  • If $c \notin \mathcal{M}$ (outside), $J_c$ is totally disconnected (Cantor dust).

In this deep sense, the Mandelbrot set is the index of all possible Julia sets.

Key Concept

The nonlinearity in $z^2$ is what creates the fractal. If we used a linear rule like $z_{n+1} = 2z_n + c$, the boundary would be a simple circle. Nonlinearity transforms a simple classification problem into infinite complexity.

What the Mandelbrot Set Looks Like

The Mandelbrot set is the black region in the classic fractal images. The colored region surrounding it represents points that escaped (diverged), colored by how many iterations they took to escape. Points that escape quickly are colored differently from points that escape slowly, creating the characteristic swirling color patterns.

The set occupies a region of the complex plane roughly spanning $[-2.5, 1.0]$ on the real axis and $[-1.2, 1.2]$ on the imaginary axis. It is symmetric about the real axis (because if $c = a + bi$ is in the set, so is $\bar{c} = a - bi$).

Self-Similarity and Infinite Detail

The boundary of the Mandelbrot set has infinite detail. Zooming into any point on the boundary reveals:

  • Miniature copies of the entire Mandelbrot set (called "baby Mandelbrots" or "satellites"), connected by delicate filaments.
  • Spiral structures that rotate and scale as you zoom deeper.
  • Seahorse tails, elephant valleys, and other named regions with characteristic visual patterns.

The deep zoom limit is set only by floating-point precision. With standard 64-bit double precision, you can zoom in by a factor of about $10^{14}$ before numerical artifacts appear. With GLSL's 32-bit float, the limit is much lower — about $10^{6}$ to $10^{7}$. Beyond these limits, arbitrary-precision (or perturbation theory) techniques are needed — and the detail continues forever, without end.

Theorem: The Mandelbrot Set Has Hausdorff Dimension 2

The boundary of the Mandelbrot set has Hausdorff dimension 2, proven by Mitsuhiro Shishikura in 1998. This means the boundary is so convoluted that it effectively fills a two-dimensional area, despite being a one-dimensional curve. No smooth (differentiable) curve can have dimension greater than 1, so this confirms that the boundary is genuinely fractal.

The Mandelbrot Set in Art and Culture

The Mandelbrot set has become an icon of mathematical beauty and a bridge between science and art. It appears in:

  • Album covers, posters, and T-shirts as a symbol of complexity from simplicity.
  • Generative art installations and real-time VJ performances.
  • Deep zoom videos on YouTube that explore the set's infinite detail, set to music.
  • Discussions of emergence, complexity science, and the nature of infinity.

For artists, the Mandelbrot set demonstrates that profound beauty can emerge from pure mathematics — no aesthetic judgment or human design is needed. The beauty is intrinsic to the mathematical structure itself. Arthur C. Clarke, the science fiction author, described the Mandelbrot set as "the most astonishing discovery in the entire history of mathematics."

In the context of computing arts, fractal rendering is a gateway to creative GPU programming. The techniques you learn here — mapping coordinates, iterating functions, choosing color palettes, writing GLSL shaders — transfer directly to other real-time visual effects: ray marching, signed distance fields, procedural textures, and audio-reactive visuals.

Testing Specific Points

Let us verify membership for several specific values of $c$:

$c = 0$: $z_0 = 0, z_1 = 0, z_2 = 0, \ldots$ — stays bounded. In the set.

$c = 1$: $z_0 = 0, z_1 = 1, z_2 = 2, z_3 = 5, z_4 = 26, \ldots$ — escapes quickly. Not in the set.

$c = -1$: $z_0 = 0, z_1 = -1, z_2 = 0, z_3 = -1, z_4 = 0, \ldots$ — oscillates between $0$ and $-1$ forever. In the set (period-2 orbit).

$c = i$: $z_0 = 0, z_1 = i, z_2 = -1+i, z_3 = -i, z_4 = -1+i, \ldots$ — enters a period-2 cycle. In the set.

$c = -2$: $z_0 = 0, z_1 = -2, z_2 = 2, z_3 = 2, \ldots$ — reaches $|z| = 2$ but does not exceed it. This is a boundary case and is technically in the set (it sits at the leftmost tip of the Mandelbrot set, called the "antenna tip").

Historical Note

Benoit Mandelbrot (1924–2010) coined the term "fractal" in 1975 and first computed images of this set in 1978–1980 at IBM Research. He showed that these shapes, far from being mathematical curiosities, describe the geometry of natural phenomena: coastlines, clouds, mountains, blood vessels, and river networks. His 1982 book The Fractal Geometry of Nature transformed our understanding of irregular forms in nature.

Knowledge Check

For $c = 0$, iterating $z_{n+1} = z_n^2 + c$ from $z_0 = 0$ gives $0, 0, 0, \ldots$. Is $c = 0$ in the Mandelbrot set?

When $c = 0$, every iteration gives $z^2 + 0 = z^2$, and starting from $z_0 = 0$ we get $0, 0, 0, \ldots$ forever. The sequence is trivially bounded, so $c = 0$ is in the Mandelbrot set — it sits at the center of the main cardioid.

Detailed Geometry of the Mandelbrot Set

The Mandelbrot set has a rich and well-studied geometry. Understanding its parts helps you navigate when zooming and exploring:

  • Main cardioid: The large, heart-shaped central region. Points here produce sequences that converge to a single fixed point. The equation for its boundary is $c = \frac{e^{i\theta}}{2} - \frac{e^{2i\theta}}{4}$ for $\theta \in [0, 2\pi)$.
  • Period-2 bulb: The large circular bulb to the left of the main cardioid, centered at $c = -1$. Points here produce sequences that oscillate between two values (period-2 orbit).
  • Period-$n$ bulbs: Smaller circular bulbs attached to the main cardioid. The period-$n$ bulb sits at the angle $\theta = 2\pi/n$ around the cardioid boundary. Inside, orbits settle into $n$-cycles.
  • Antenna (spike): The thin filament extending to the left, along the real axis from $c = -2$ to the period-2 bulb. This is also called the "real axis spike."
  • Seahorse valley: The narrow region between the main cardioid and the period-2 bulb (near $c = -0.75$). Zooming here reveals seahorse-shaped spiral structures.
  • Elephant valley: The region along the positive imaginary boundary of the main cardioid. Zooming reveals elephant-trunk-like spiral structures.
  • Mini-Mandelbrots: Tiny copies of the entire Mandelbrot set appear throughout the boundary, each slightly distorted and surrounded by unique filaments. These are not approximate copies — they are exact topological replicas.

The most visually spectacular zooming locations are near the boundary, especially at Misiurewicz points (pre-periodic points) where the local geometry mimics a Julia set, and at the "cusps" between bulbs where spiral structures of extraordinary intricacy emerge.

The Mandelbrot set is connected (proven by Douady and Hubbard in 1982), meaning all of its parts are joined together, even though some connections are vanishingly thin filaments. It is also simply connected, meaning it has no holes. These topological properties were not obvious from early computer images and required sophisticated mathematical proof.

One of the deepest unsolved problems about the Mandelbrot set is the MLC conjecture (Mandelbrot set is Locally Connected), which if true would imply a complete combinatorial classification of all the dynamics within the set. This conjecture remains one of the central open problems in complex dynamics.

3.8 Implementing Mandelbrot on CPU

The strategy for a CPU implementation:

  1. For each pixel, determine the corresponding complex number $c$.
  2. Iterate $z = z^2 + c$ starting from $z = 0$.
  3. Count iterations until $|z| > 2$ (or reach a maximum).
  4. Color the pixel based on the iteration count.

Complex squaring is $(a+bi)^2 = (a^2 - b^2) + 2abi$, not $(a^2, b^2)$. Component-wise squaring gives a completely wrong result. Always use the correct formula.

Understanding the Color Mapping

The coloring function used in the code above deserves explanation. Points in the set (iteration count equals maxIter) are colored black. Points outside the set are colored based on how quickly they escaped, using Bernstein polynomial basis functions:

let t = iteration / maxIter;  // normalize to [0, 1]
R = floor(9 * (1-t) * t*t*t * 255);     // peaks late
G = floor(15 * (1-t)*(1-t) * t*t * 255); // peaks in middle
B = floor(8.5 * (1-t)*(1-t)*(1-t) * t * 255); // peaks early

These are cubic Bernstein polynomials $B_{i,3}(t) = \binom{3}{i} (1-t)^{3-i} t^i$ with scaling factors. The red channel peaks for large $t$ (slow escape), green peaks in the middle, and blue peaks for small $t$ (fast escape). The result is a palette that transitions from deep blue (fast escape, far from set) through green and yellow to dark red (slow escape, near the boundary).

You can create your own palettes by changing these polynomial coefficients, or by using entirely different coloring schemes (HSB mode, cosine palettes, histogram equalization, etc.).

For a 300x300 canvas with maxIter = 80, we perform up to $300 \times 300 \times 80 = 7{,}200{,}000$ complex multiplications. Each multiplication involves 4 real multiplications, 1 addition, and 1 subtraction. The bottleneck is serial processing: all 90,000 pixels are computed one at a time by a single CPU thread.

Optimizations that help on CPU:

  • Avoid math.js — manual arithmetic eliminates object allocation overhead.
  • Use $|z|^2 > 4$ instead of $|z| > 2$ to avoid sqrt().
  • Use pixelDensity(1) to avoid computing extra pixels on high-DPI screens.
  • Reduce canvas size (the GPU version eliminates this constraint).

Even with these optimizations, CPU rendering remains impractical for interactive exploration. This is the fundamental motivation for porting the algorithm to the GPU in Section 3.12.

Coordinate Mapping: From Pixels to Complex Numbers

One of the most important concepts in fractal rendering is the coordinate mapping from pixel space to the complex plane. The canvas has pixel coordinates $(px, py)$ with $px \in [0, \text{width})$ and $py \in [0, \text{height})$. The complex plane has coordinates $(re, im)$ in some viewing rectangle.

The mapping is a linear transformation:

$$re = \text{realMin} + \frac{px}{\text{width}} \cdot (\text{realMax} - \text{realMin})$$

$$im = \text{imagMax} - \frac{py}{\text{height}} \cdot (\text{imagMax} - \text{imagMin})$$

Note the y-axis flip: pixel $y$ increases downward, but we want the imaginary axis to increase upward. This is handled by mapping from imagMax down to imagMin as py increases. In p5.js, the map() function handles this. In GLSL, the mix() function does the equivalent: mix(a, b, t) = a*(1-t) + b*t.

To implement zooming, you simply change the viewing rectangle. To zoom in by a factor of $k$ centered on a point $(x_c, y_c)$:

let halfW = (realMax - realMin) / (2 * k);
let halfH = (imagMax - imagMin) / (2 * k);
realMin = xc - halfW;  realMax = xc + halfW;
imagMin = yc - halfH;  imagMax = yc + halfH;

3.9 Julia Sets

While the Mandelbrot set varies $c$ and fixes $z_0 = 0$, Julia sets do the opposite: they fix $c$ and vary the starting point $z_0$ across the complex plane.

Definition

Filled Julia Set — For a fixed complex number $c$, the filled Julia set $K_c$ is the set of all starting points $z_0$ for which $z_{n+1} = z_n^2 + c$ does not diverge. The Julia set $J_c$ is the boundary of $K_c$.

Connected vs. Disconnected

  • If $c \in \mathcal{M}$ (inside the Mandelbrot set), the Julia set is connected — one continuous curve.
  • If $c \notin \mathcal{M}$ (outside), the Julia set is totally disconnected — a Cantor dust of infinitely many isolated points.

This is the Mandelbrot-Julia correspondence: the Mandelbrot set classifies all Julia sets by their topological type.

3.10 Newton's Method and Root Finding

Newton's method is an iterative algorithm for finding zeros (roots) of a function — values where $f(x) = 0$. It connects deeply to our theme of iteration and, when applied to complex-valued functions, produces beautiful fractals.

The Iteration Formula

Given a differentiable function $f$ whose root we seek (a value $x^*$ where $f(x^*) = 0$), Newton's method generates successive approximations:

$$x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}$$

where $f'(x_n)$ is the derivative of $f$ evaluated at the current guess. The idea is that near a root, the tangent line is a good approximation to the function, so following the tangent to the x-axis gives a much better approximation.

Geometric Intuition

At each step, Newton's method:

  1. Evaluates the function at the current guess $x_n$.
  2. Draws the tangent line to the curve at that point.
  3. Finds where the tangent crosses the x-axis.
  4. Uses that crossing as the next guess $x_{n+1}$.

If the function is smooth and the initial guess is reasonable, convergence is remarkably fast. Newton's method has quadratic convergence: the number of correct digits roughly doubles with each iteration. Starting from 1 correct digit, after just 10 iterations you may have $2^{10} \approx 1000$ correct digits.

Theorem: Quadratic Convergence of Newton's Method

If $f$ is twice continuously differentiable, $f(x^*) = 0$, and $f'(x^*) \neq 0$, then for starting points sufficiently close to $x^*$, Newton's method converges quadratically: $$|x_{n+1} - x^*| \leq C |x_n - x^*|^2$$ for some constant $C = \frac{|f''(x^*)|}{2|f'(x^*)|}$. The error is squared at each step.

Example: Finding $\sqrt{2}$

To compute $\sqrt{2}$, we find a root of $f(x) = x^2 - 2$. The derivative is $f'(x) = 2x$, so the iteration is:

$$x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{x_n^2 - 2}{2x_n} = \frac{x_n + 2/x_n}{2}$$

Starting from $x_0 = 1$:

  • $x_1 = \frac{1 + 2}{2} = 1.5$
  • $x_2 = \frac{1.5 + 1.333...}{2} = 1.41\overline{6}$
  • $x_3 = 1.414215686...$
  • $x_4 = 1.414213562373...$

After just 4 iterations, we have 12 correct digits. This is quadratic convergence in action — the Babylonians used this exact algorithm 4000 years ago to compute square roots, though they did not have the formal framework of calculus to explain why it works.

Historical Note

Newton himself described the method in De analysi (1669), but did not write it as a general iteration formula. The modern formulation as $x_{n+1} = x_n - f(x_n)/f'(x_n)$ is due to Joseph Raphson (1690), which is why the method is sometimes called the Newton-Raphson method. The graphical interpretation using tangent lines came even later, from Thomas Simpson in the 1740s.

Newton's Method for Other Functions

Newton's method works for any differentiable function. Here are some useful examples:

  • Cube root of $a$: Solve $f(x) = x^3 - a$. The iteration is $x_{n+1} = \frac{2x_n^3 + a}{3x_n^2}$.
  • Reciprocal of $a$ (division without dividing): Solve $f(x) = 1/x - a$. The iteration is $x_{n+1} = x_n(2 - ax_n)$. This is used in hardware floating-point division circuits.
  • Inverse square root: Solve $f(x) = 1/x^2 - a$. The iteration is $x_{n+1} = \frac{x_n}{2}(3 - ax_n^2)$. This is related to the famous "fast inverse square root" algorithm from the Quake III Arena source code.
  • Solving $\cos(x) = x$: Solve $f(x) = \cos(x) - x$. The iteration is $x_{n+1} = x_n + \frac{\cos(x_n) - x_n}{\sin(x_n) + 1}$, which converges to the Dottie number $\approx 0.739085$.

Newton Fractals

When applied to functions of complex numbers, Newton's method reveals fractal structure. Consider $f(z) = z^3 - 1$, which has three cube roots of unity:

$$z_1 = 1, \qquad z_2 = -\tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i, \qquad z_3 = -\tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i$$

The Newton iteration becomes:

$$z_{n+1} = z_n - \frac{z_n^3 - 1}{3z_n^2} = \frac{2z_n^3 + 1}{3z_n^2}$$

Color each starting point by which root it converges to, and you get a fractal. The boundaries between the three colored regions are infinitely complex — every boundary point is a point of sensitive dependence where a tiny change in the starting position can change which root Newton's method finds.

The Newton fractal for $z^3 - 1$ has threefold rotational symmetry, reflecting the equal angular spacing of the three cube roots (120 degrees apart). The fractal boundary consists of infinitely many "tentacles" where the three basins of attraction interweave at every scale.

Why Newton's Method Can Fail

Newton's method is not guaranteed to converge. It can:

  • Diverge: If $f'(x_n) = 0$ at some iterate, the formula divides by zero.
  • Cycle: The iteration can enter a periodic orbit, never reaching any root.
  • Converge to the "wrong" root: Starting near one root does not guarantee convergence to that root. This is precisely what creates the fractal structure.

The basins of attraction (the sets of starting points converging to each root) have fractal boundaries, meaning that near any boundary point, all three basins are interleaved at arbitrarily fine scales. This has practical consequences: in numerical software, Newton’s method may behave unpredictably near these boundaries.

Implementing a Newton Fractal

A Newton fractal can be rendered with the same GPU approach as the Mandelbrot set. The key differences:

  • The starting point $z_0$ varies per pixel (not $c$).
  • The iteration formula is $z_{n+1} = z_n - f(z_n)/f'(z_n)$ instead of $z^2 + c$.
  • Coloring is based on which root the iteration converges to, not on escape count.
  • Complex division is needed: $\frac{a+bi}{c+di} = \frac{(ac+bd) + (bc-ad)i}{c^2+d^2}$.

Newton fractals produce striking three-colored (or more) images that complement the Mandelbrot set’s two-state classification. They are particularly beautiful when the iteration count is also used to modulate brightness, creating a 3D-like shading effect on the basins.

Key Concept

Newton fractals reveal that even well-behaved numerical algorithms can have fractal basins of attraction. The "simple" question "which root will Newton's method find?" has an answer of infinite complexity.

Newton's Method for Real Functions: A Code Example

Here is Newton's method applied to $f(x) = x^3 + 2x^2 + 1$, with derivative $f'(x) = 3x^2 + 4x$:

function setup() {
  createCanvas(400, 400);
  let x = 1.2;  // initial guess
  for (let i = 0; i < 20; i++) {
    console.log('x_' + i + ' = ' + x.toFixed(12));
    x = x - (x*x*x + 2*x*x + 1) / (3*x*x + 4*x);

}

Running this produces rapid convergence. Within about 6 iterations, the value stabilizes to 12+ decimal places — a dramatic demonstration of quadratic convergence.

3.11 Introduction to GLSL

The CPU Mandelbrot takes many seconds. But the algorithm is embarrassingly parallel — every pixel's computation is independent. The GPU, with its thousands of cores, can compute all pixels simultaneously.

The Shader Pipeline

GLSL (OpenGL Shading Language) is a C-like programming language for GPU shaders. A program has two stages:

  1. Vertex shader: Runs once per vertex. Positions geometry on screen.
  2. Fragment shader: Runs once per pixel (fragment). Determines each pixel's color.

For fractals, the vertex shader is trivial — it just passes geometry positions through. All the interesting work happens in the fragment shader, which determines the color of each pixel independently.

How GPU Parallelism Works

A modern GPU contains hundreds to thousands of small processing cores organized into groups called warps (NVIDIA) or wavefronts (AMD). When you draw a full-screen quad, the GPU dispatches one thread per pixel. For a 400x400 canvas, that is 160,000 threads running simultaneously (or in very rapid succession on the available cores).

Each thread executes the same fragment shader code but with different inputs (different pixel positions). This is called SIMD (Single Instruction, Multiple Data) or SIMT (Single Instruction, Multiple Threads) architecture. The GPU excels when all threads do similar work — which is exactly what happens in fractal rendering, where every pixel runs the same iteration loop.

Key Concept

The Mandelbrot algorithm is embarrassingly parallel: each pixel's computation is completely independent of every other pixel. There are no data dependencies, no shared state, no synchronization needed. This is the ideal case for GPU computing and explains why the speedup is so dramatic.

Key Concepts

  • Uniforms: Values sent from JavaScript to the shader, constant across all pixels. Example: u_time, u_mouse, u_resolution.
  • Varyings: Values interpolated from vertex shader to fragment shader across the surface.
  • Attributes: Per-vertex data (position, normals, etc.).

Setting Up Shaders in p5.js

To use GLSL shaders in p5.js, you need three things: a canvas in WEBGL mode, a vertex shader source string, and a fragment shader source string:

let s; // shader variable

function setup() {
  createCanvas(400, 400, WEBGL); // must use WEBGL mode
  s = createShader(vertexSource, fragmentSource);
  noStroke();
}

function draw() {
  shader(s); // activate the shader

  // Send data from JavaScript to the shader
  s.setUniform("u_time", millis() / 1000.0);
  s.setUniform("u_resolution", [width, height]);
  s.setUniform("u_mouse", [mouseX/width, mouseY/height]);

  // Draw a quad covering the entire canvas
  quad(-1, -1, 1, -1, 1, 1, -1, 1);
}

Key points:

  • WEBGL mode is required for shader support.
  • createShader() compiles the vertex and fragment shader source and returns a shader object.
  • shader(s) activates the shader for all subsequent drawing.
  • setUniform() sends values from JavaScript (CPU) to the shader (GPU). This is the communication bridge.
  • The quad(-1,-1,1,-1,1,1,-1,1) draws a full-screen rectangle in clip space coordinates, ensuring every pixel is processed.

The Vertex Shader

For fractal rendering, the vertex shader is minimal — it passes positions through without transformation:

precision highp float;
attribute vec3 aPosition;
varying vec2 vPosition;

void main() {
  gl_Position = vec4(aPosition, 1.0);
  vPosition = gl_Position.xy;
}

The varying keyword means vPosition is automatically interpolated across the surface of the quad, so each fragment (pixel) receives a smoothly varying position value. At the four corners of the quad, vPosition has the exact vertex positions: $(-1,-1)$, $(1,-1)$, $(1,1)$, $(-1,1)$. Between the corners, the GPU interpolates linearly, creating a smooth gradient of positions.

The three types of shader variables form a communication chain:

  • Attributes (attribute) carry data into the vertex shader from JavaScript (per-vertex data like positions)
  • Varyings (varying) carry data from the vertex shader to the fragment shader (automatically interpolated)
  • Uniforms (uniform) carry data from JavaScript to either shader (constant across all vertices/pixels in a frame)

One of GLSL's most distinctive features is swizzling: accessing and rearranging vector components using combinations of .x, .y, .z, .w (or equivalently .r, .g, .b, .a):

Swizzling is extremely common in shader code and contributes to its compact, expressive style. The last pattern — vec4(vec3(x), 1.0) — is used in almost every fragment shader to convert a color value to an RGBA output.

Not all mobile GPUs support highp in fragment shaders. If your fractal looks blocky on a phone or tablet, the device may be silently falling back to mediump. You can check for high precision support by testing GL_FRAGMENT_PRECISION_HIGH in the shader.

GLSL Gotchas

Common Mistake

Integer/float strictness: GLSL does not automatically convert between int and float. You must write 1.0 instead of 1, vec2(1.0, 1.0) not vec2(1, 1), and 7.0 / 10.0 not 7 / 10. This causes more errors than anything else when learning GLSL.

Other important gotchas:

  • No print/debug: The only output is the pixel color. Debug by outputting values as colors: gl_FragColor = vec4(value, 0.0, 0.0, 1.0);
  • Constant loop bounds: Some GPUs require for loop bounds to be compile-time constants. Use a fixed maximum and break early.
  • No recursion: GLSL does not support recursive function calls.
  • No dynamic arrays: All array sizes must be known at compile time.
  • No strings: GLSL has no string type. All text rendering must be done on the CPU side.
  • Limited branching: if/else statements work but can reduce performance because all threads in a warp must execute both branches (this is called divergent branching). Use step(), mix(), and smoothstep() for branchless alternatives when possible.

Since there is no console.log() in GLSL, the standard debugging technique is to output the value you want to inspect as a pixel color:

The fragment shader runs once per pixel and knows its position. The Mandelbrot algorithm computes one pixel given its position. These are a perfect match — the GPU handles the outer pixel loop by running the fragment shader on all pixels simultaneously.

Complex Numbers as vec2

We represent complex numbers as vec2: vec2(real, imag). Addition works automatically (component-wise vec2 + vec2 matches complex addition). Multiplication requires a custom function:

vec2 cmul(vec2 a, vec2 b) {
  return vec2(a.x*b.x - a.y*b.y,
              a.x*b.y + a.y*b.x);
}
Common Mistake

Do not use z * z for complex squaring! In GLSL, * on vec2 is component-wise: vec2(a*a, b*b). Complex squaring should give vec2(a*a - b*b, 2.0*a*b). For example, vec2(2,3) * vec2(2,3) gives vec2(4,9), but the correct answer for $(2+3i)^2$ is $-5+12i$.

Understanding the GPU Mandelbrot Shader

Let us walk through the key lines of the fragment shader:

Line 6-8: cmul function — Implements complex multiplication. The real part is $a_r b_r - a_i b_i$ and the imaginary part is $a_r b_i + a_i b_r$. This function replaces the math.multiply() calls from the CPU version.

Line 12: UV coordinates — gl_FragCoord.xy gives the pixel position in screen coordinates (0 to width, 0 to height). Dividing by u_resolution normalizes to $[0, 1]$.

Lines 15-18: mix() for coordinate mapping — The mix(a, b, t) function computes $a(1-t) + bt$, which linearly interpolates from $a$ to $b$. When uv.x = 0, c.x = -2.5; when uv.x = 1, c.x = 1.0. This replaces the map() function from p5.js.

Line 24: Fixed loop bound — GLSL on some GPUs requires loop bounds to be compile-time constants. We use 200 as a fixed upper limit. On more advanced GPUs, you can use a uniform integer, but the constant approach is the most portable.

Line 25: The iteration — cmul(z, z) + c computes $z^2 + c$. Note that + c uses automatic vec2 addition, which correctly adds the real and imaginary parts.

Lines 32-38: Cosine color palette — The formula 0.5 + 0.5*cos(6.2832*(t*3.0 + offset)) creates a smooth, cyclic color palette. The three different offsets ($0.0$, $0.33$, $0.67$) produce different phase shifts for R, G, and B, creating a rainbow-like effect. The factor of $3.0$ on $t$ makes the colors cycle three times across the iteration range.

The Speedup

The GPU version renders in roughly $1/60$ second — fast enough for real-time 60 fps animation. The CPU version takes many seconds. That is a speedup of roughly 3,600x. This comes entirely from parallelism: each GPU core is actually slower than a CPU core, but thousands run simultaneously.

This speedup makes possible things that are impossible on CPU: real-time zooming, panning, and animation of fractal parameters. It also enables rendering at high resolution (1080p or 4K) without waiting minutes per frame.

Extending the Shader

To create different fractals, change the iteration rule in the fragment shader:

// z^3 + c (Multibrot)
z = cmul(cmul(z, z), z) + c;

// Burning Ship fractal
z = vec2(abs(z.x), abs(z.y));
z = cmul(z, z) + c;

// Tricorn (Mandelbar)
z = vec2(z.x, -z.y);  // conjugate
z = cmul(z, z) + c;

Each produces a dramatically different fractal with its own aesthetic character. You will also need to adjust the bounds (mix parameters) to frame the interesting region.

Adding Zoom and Pan

To create an interactive zooming Mandelbrot, send the view bounds as uniforms from JavaScript:

// In JavaScript draw():
s.setUniform("bounds_min", [centerX - zoom, centerY - zoom]);

// On mouse click, zoom in:
function mousePressed() {
  centerX = map(mouseX, 0, width, centerX - zoom, centerX + zoom);
  centerY = map(mouseY, 0, height, centerY + zoom, centerY - zoom);
  zoom *= 0.5;  // zoom in by factor of 2
}

With each click, the view zooms by a factor of 2, revealing more detail. Because the GPU renders in real-time, you can explore the Mandelbrot set interactively — something impossible with the CPU version.

Smooth Coloring (Normalized Iteration Count)

The standard iteration-count coloring produces visible "bands" where the iteration count jumps by 1. A smoother coloring technique uses the normalized iteration count:

// In the fragment shader, after the loop:
float smoothIter = float(iter) + 1.0
    - log(log(length(z))) / log(2.0);
float t = smoothIter / float(maxIter);

This formula interpolates fractionally between iteration bands based on how far past the escape radius the point traveled, producing silky-smooth gradients instead of hard color boundaries. The mathematical basis is the potential function (Green's function) of the Mandelbrot set, which measures the asymptotic growth rate of the orbit.

Distance Estimation

An even more advanced technique is distance estimation, which approximates the distance from each exterior point to the Mandelbrot set boundary. The distance estimate uses the derivative of the iteration with respect to $c$:

$$d(c) \approx \frac{|z_n| \cdot \ln|z_n|}{|z'_n|}$$

where $z'_n$ is computed alongside $z_n$ using the chain rule: $z'_{n+1} = 2z_n z'_n + 1$, with $z'_0 = 0$. Distance estimation enables anti-aliased rendering, boundary glow effects, and 3D-like visualizations. We explore this in Chapter 4.

Color Palettes for Fractal Art

The cosine palette used in the GPU version is one of many possible color mapping techniques:

  • Linear gradient: Map iteration count directly to a color ramp. Simple but produces visible banding.
  • Cosine palette: col = 0.5 + 0.5*cos(2*PI*(t*freq + offset)). Smooth and cyclic, with easy parameter control.
  • HSB mapping: Map iteration to hue, with saturation and brightness fixed or modulated. Produces rainbow effects.
  • Histogram equalization: Distribute colors so that each color appears equally often, maximizing visual contrast.
  • Orbit traps: Color based on how close the orbit passes to some geometric shape (a point, line, or circle). Creates unexpected patterns.

The choice of palette is where mathematics meets art. The same fractal data can look dramatically different — serene or psychedelic, subtle or bold — depending on the color mapping.

Key Concept

The central theme: simple rules, iterated with feedback, produce infinite complexity. A single line of math ($z^2 + c$) generates a shape with more detail than any finite description can capture. This is the deep connection between chaos theory, fractal geometry, and computational art.

Recurrence relationAn equation defining each term of a sequence in terms of previous terms: $x_{n+1} = f(x_n, x_{n-1}, \ldots)$.

SequenceAn ordered list of numbers: $a_1, a_2, a_3, \ldots$

Chapter 3 Review Quiz

Test your understanding of iteration, chaos, complex numbers, and fractals.

Question 1

The harmonic series $1 + \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{3} + \tfrac{1}{4} + \cdots$:

The harmonic series is the classic example of a divergent series whose terms approach zero. The partial sums grow without bound (albeit very slowly). This demonstrates that the divergence test's converse is false.

Question 2

The Feigenbaum constant is approximately:

The Feigenbaum constant $\delta \approx 4.669201\ldots$ is the limiting ratio of successive bifurcation intervals in the period-doubling route to chaos. It is universal across all one-dimensional maps that undergo period doubling.

Question 3

A point $c$ is in the Mandelbrot set if:

By definition, $c \in \mathcal{M}$ if and only if the iteration $z_{n+1} = z_n^2 + c$, starting from $z_0 = 0$, produces a bounded sequence. "Bounded" means $|z_n|$ never exceeds some finite value.

Question 4

If $c$ is outside the Mandelbrot set, its Julia set is:

The Mandelbrot-Julia correspondence states: $c$ inside $\mathcal{M}$ gives a connected Julia set; $c$ outside $\mathcal{M}$ gives a totally disconnected Julia set (Cantor dust). The Mandelbrot set is the "index" classifying Julia sets by their topology.

Question 5

Why must we use a custom function for complex multiplication in GLSL?

In GLSL, vec2 * vec2 is component-wise: vec2(a*c, b*d). Complex multiplication requires cross-terms: vec2(a*c - b*d, a*d + b*c). These are completely different! For example, $(2+3i)^2 = -5+12i$, but component-wise gives $(4, 9)$.
