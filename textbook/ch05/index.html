MAT 200C: Computing Arts — Self-referencing systems and mathematical shape description

5.1 Introduction

In the previous chapter we explored fractals — shapes that contain copies of themselves at every scale, generated through mathematical iteration. In this chapter we encounter two techniques that are equally fundamental to creative computing but operate in very different ways: frame feedback and signed distance functions. At first glance these topics may seem unrelated, but they share a deep connection: both are techniques for building visual complexity from simple, self-referential processes.

Frame feedback creates complexity through temporal self-reference. Each frame of animation feeds back into the next, accumulating transformations over time. A simple circle, drawn once, becomes a spiraling tunnel of echoes. A small perturbation becomes an intricate fractal-like pattern. The image remembers its past and uses that memory as the raw material for its future.

Signed distance functions (SDFs) create complexity through spatial self-reference. An SDF describes a shape not by listing its pixels or vertices, but by defining, for every point in space, how far that point is from the shape’s boundary. This single, continuous function encodes everything about the shape — its interior, exterior, and boundary — and can be combined with other SDFs through simple arithmetic to build elaborate composite forms.

💡 Key Concept

Self-reference as a creative engine. Frame feedback feeds the output of one frame into the input of the next. SDFs describe shapes in terms of distance — a relationship between a point and a boundary. Both techniques derive their power from defining things in terms of themselves or their relationships, rather than by explicit construction. This is the same principle that makes fractals and recursion so visually rich.

These two techniques also represent different stages of the graphics pipeline. Frame feedback is fundamentally about pixel-level persistence — reading and writing to a framebuffer over time. Signed distance functions are about shape description — encoding geometry as a mathematical function that can be evaluated per-pixel in a shader. When you combine both — drawing SDF shapes into a feedback system — you get a remarkably expressive creative instrument with very little code.

5.2 A Brief History of Video Feedback Art

Before we dive into the technical details, it is worth understanding where frame feedback comes from. The technique has its roots in one of the most influential movements in twentieth-century art: video art.

Video feedback was discovered almost by accident, shortly after closed-circuit television became available in the 1960s. When a video camera is pointed at the monitor displaying its own output, a visual feedback loop is created. The image the camera captures includes the image on the screen, which includes the image the camera captured a moment ago, which includes the image from a moment before that — an infinite regression of images nested within images. The result is a swirling, often psychedelic pattern that seems to have a life of its own.

📜 Historical Note

Nam June Paik (1932–2006) is widely regarded as the founder of video art. A Korean-American artist who began as a musician and performance artist in the Fluxus movement, Paik was among the first to use television as an artistic medium. His installations, such as TV Buddha (1974) — in which a Buddha statue contemplates its own closed-circuit image on a television screen — explored the philosophical implications of electronic self-reference. Paik recognized that video feedback was not merely a technical curiosity but a metaphor for consciousness, memory, and the recursive nature of perception.

Steina and Woody Vasulka were pioneers of electronic image processing who made video feedback central to their artistic practice throughout the 1970s and 80s. Working at The Kitchen in New York City — an artist-run space they co-founded — the Vasulkas treated the video signal as a material to be sculpted. They built custom electronic circuits to transform the feedback signal in real time, producing works of extraordinary visual complexity. Steina’s Allvision series used mirrored spheres and rotating cameras to create feedback environments that enveloped the viewer. Woody’s investigations into “the grammar of electronic image” laid theoretical groundwork for understanding how electronic signals produce visual form.

What made video feedback so compelling to these artists was its emergent quality. Small changes in the physical setup — tilting the camera slightly, adjusting the zoom, changing the brightness — produced dramatic and unpredictable changes in the pattern. The system was exquisitely sensitive to initial conditions, much like the chaotic systems we studied in earlier chapters. The artist became a performer, tuning a self-organizing visual system rather than drawing predetermined images.

Digital frame feedback gives us the same creative power with far greater precision. Instead of physically adjusting a camera and monitor, we specify exact mathematical transformations: a rotation of 0.015 radians, a scale factor of 0.995, a decay rate of 0.98. We can reproduce any state exactly, vary parameters smoothly, and combine feedback with other techniques like SDFs and shaders. The creative spirit of Paik and the Vasulkas lives on in every frame feedback sketch.

5.3 Framebuffers

To understand frame feedback, we first need to understand framebuffers — the technical infrastructure that makes feedback possible.

📖 Definition

Framebuffer — A region of memory (typically on the GPU) that holds the pixel data for a complete image. Your screen displays one framebuffer — the “default” or “screen” framebuffer. When we create additional framebuffers, we get off-screen surfaces that can be drawn to and read from independently. Think of a framebuffer as a secondary canvas that exists in GPU memory but is not directly visible until you choose to display it.

Normally when you call drawing commands in p5.js, you draw directly to the screen. The pixels you produce are immediately visible. But sometimes you need to draw to an off-screen surface — to capture an image for later use, to apply post-processing effects, or to create feedback loops. This is what framebuffers are for.

In p5.js with WEBGL mode, the function createFramebuffer() creates an off-screen framebuffer object:

let fbo;

function setup() {
  createCanvas(600, 600, WEBGL);
  fbo = createFramebuffer();
}

The framebuffer fbo is now a secondary drawing surface with the same dimensions as your canvas. You can draw into it by wrapping your drawing commands between fbo.begin() and fbo.end():

fbo.begin();
// Everything drawn here goes into the framebuffer, not the screen
fill(255, 0, 0);
circle(0, 0, 100);
fbo.end();

// Now display the framebuffer's contents on screen
texture(fbo);
noStroke();
plane(width, height);

The critical capability of a framebuffer for feedback is that it persists between frames. Unlike the main canvas, which is typically cleared with background() every frame, a framebuffer retains its pixel data from the previous frame unless you explicitly clear it. This persistence is what makes feedback possible: you can read the previous frame’s image from the framebuffer and draw a transformed version of it back into the same framebuffer.

⚠ Common Mistake

Framebuffers in p5.js require WEBGL mode. If you create your canvas with createCanvas(600, 600) (no third argument), you are in 2D mode and createFramebuffer() will not work. Always use createCanvas(600, 600, WEBGL) when working with framebuffers. Also note that in WEBGL mode, the coordinate origin is at the center of the canvas, not the top-left corner.

5.4 The Frame Feedback Loop

Now we arrive at the core technique. Frame feedback is a process in which the output of one frame becomes part of the input for the next frame. The image feeds back into itself, accumulating transformations over time. The result is a visual system with memory — the current frame depends on every frame that came before it.

The feedback loop has five steps that repeat every frame:

  1. Begin drawing into the framebuffer
  2. Draw the framebuffer’s own previous contents back into itself, with a transformation applied (scale, rotation, fade)
  3. Draw new content on top (shapes, lines, particles — whatever you want to add this frame)
  4. End drawing into the framebuffer
  5. Display the framebuffer on screen

Step 2 is where the magic happens. By drawing the previous frame back into the current frame with a slight transformation, every pixel from the previous frame is captured, shifted, and preserved. New content drawn in step 3 gets added to this accumulation. On the next frame, that new content itself gets transformed and preserved, creating a chain of echoes stretching back through time.

💡 Key Concept

The five-step feedback pattern. Begin → draw previous frame with transform → draw new content → end → display. This pattern is the foundation of all frame feedback systems. The character of the visual output is determined by the transformation applied in step 2 (scale, rotation, decay) and the content drawn in step 3. Memorize this pattern — you will use it throughout this chapter and beyond.

The character of the feedback is controlled by three parameters:

Decay rate: How quickly old content fades away. In p5.js, this is controlled by the tint() alpha value. A tint alpha of 255 means no fade at all — the image never loses brightness, and will eventually saturate to white. This is almost never what you want. An alpha of 240 produces rapid fade (short trails). Values around 248–253 give pleasingly long trails that eventually disappear. The decay rate determines the temporal depth of your feedback — how many frames back in history are still visible.

Spatial transformation: What geometric change is applied to the previous frame. The most common transformations are:

  • Scale > 1: Content zooms outward — the image appears to recede into a tunnel
  • Scale < 1: Content zooms inward — creating a vortex or shrinking spiral
  • Rotation: Content spirals around the center
  • Translation: Content drifts in a consistent direction

New content: What you draw each frame. Simple shapes work remarkably well because the feedback itself adds complexity. A single small circle becomes an infinite spiral of echoes. A line becomes a fan of converging or diverging rays. Resist the temptation to draw complex shapes — let the feedback do the work.

5.5 The Rotation Matrix

The feedback examples above use p5.js’s built-in rotate() function, which handles the mathematics for us. But when we move to GLSL shaders, we need to construct the rotation ourselves. This is one of the most important mathematical tools in computer graphics, and it is worth understanding how it works from first principles.

5.5.1 Deriving the 2D Rotation Matrix

Consider a point $(x, y)$ in the plane. We want to rotate it by angle $\theta$ around the origin to get a new point $(x', y')$. To derive the formula, we express the point in polar coordinates: $x = r\cos\alpha$ and $y = r\sin\alpha$, where $r$ is the distance from the origin and $\alpha$ is the current angle.

After rotation by $\theta$, the new angle is $\alpha + \theta$, and the distance $r$ stays the same (rotation does not change distance). So:

$$x' = r\cos(\alpha + \theta) = r(\cos\alpha\cos\theta - \sin\alpha\sin\theta) = x\cos\theta - y\sin\theta$$

$$y' = r\sin(\alpha + \theta) = r(\sin\alpha\cos\theta + \cos\alpha\sin\theta) = x\sin\theta + y\cos\theta$$

We can write this as a matrix multiplication:

$$\begin{pmatrix} x' \\ y' \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}$$

This $2 \times 2$ matrix is the rotation matrix. It has several important properties:

  • It preserves distances: $|p'| = |p|$ (rotation is an isometry)
  • It preserves angles between vectors
  • Its determinant is 1 (no scaling or flipping)
  • Its inverse is its transpose (or equivalently, negate $\theta$)
  • Composing two rotations is equivalent to adding their angles

5.5.2 The Rotation Matrix in GLSL

In GLSL, we construct the rotation matrix using mat2. There is a subtlety here that catches many beginners: GLSL fills matrices column by column, not row by row. The constructor mat2(a, b, c, d) creates:

$$\begin{pmatrix} a & c \\ b & d \end{pmatrix}$$

So to create our rotation matrix $\begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$, we write:

float c = cos(angle);
float s = sin(angle);
mat2 rot = mat2(c, s, -s, c);

Note the order: mat2(c, s, -s, c) — not mat2(c, -s, s, c). The first two arguments fill the first column $(c, s)$, and the second two fill the second column $(-s, c)$. This gives us the matrix we want.

⚠ Common Mistake

GLSL matrix column-major order. The most common rotation matrix bug is writing mat2(cos(a), -sin(a), sin(a), cos(a)) — which looks like the mathematical notation but is wrong in GLSL because the arguments fill columns, not rows. The correct form is mat2(cos(a), sin(a), -sin(a), cos(a)). If your rotation goes in the wrong direction, this is almost certainly the problem.

To combine rotation with scaling (as we need for feedback), simply multiply the sine and cosine by the scale factor:

vec2 scaleRotate(vec2 p, float scale, float angle) {
  float c = cos(angle) * scale;
  float s = sin(angle) * scale;
  return mat2(c, s, -s, c) * p;
}

This single matrix simultaneously rotates and scales, which is exactly the transformation we need for video feedback.

✍ Knowledge Check

In GLSL, mat2(a, b, c, d) constructs a matrix with columns (a,b) and (c,d). Which of the following creates a correct 2D rotation matrix for angle t?

Since GLSL fills matrices column-by-column, mat2(cos(t), sin(t), -sin(t), cos(t)) creates columns $(cos, sin)$ and $(-sin, cos)$, yielding the rotation matrix $\begin{pmatrix} \cos t & -\sin t \\ \sin t & \cos t \end{pmatrix}$.

5.6 Frame Feedback in GLSL

The p5.js feedback loop we built in Section 5.4 works well, but it has limitations. The tint() and scale() functions apply uniform transformations to the entire image. For more creative control — per-pixel distortions, color channel separation, non-linear warping — we need to implement the feedback transformation in a fragment shader.

The idea is the same as before: sample the previous frame’s image at transformed coordinates, apply decay, and output the result. But instead of using p5.js transforms, we do the coordinate transformation ourselves in GLSL, giving us complete control over every aspect of the process.

5.6.1 The Basic GLSL Feedback Shader

In a GLSL feedback shader, the previous frame is passed as a texture uniform (typically called u_feedback or u_backbuffer). The shader samples this texture at transformed UV coordinates to create the feedback effect. Here is a basic example:

precision mediump float;

varying vec2 vTexCoord;
uniform sampler2D u_feedback;
uniform float u_time;

void main() {
  vec2 uv = vTexCoord;
  vec2 center = uv - 0.5;

  // Rotation
  float angle = 0.015;
  float c = cos(angle);
  float s = sin(angle);
  center = mat2(c, s, -s, c) * center;

  // Scale (slight zoom inward)
  center *= 0.995;

  // Sample previous frame at transformed coordinates
  vec2 feedUV = center + 0.5;
  vec4 prev = texture2D(u_feedback, feedUV);

  // Decay
  prev *= 0.985;

  gl_FragColor = prev;
}

This shader does the same thing as our p5.js feedback loop — rotate, scale, and fade — but with per-pixel precision. Each pixel independently computes where in the previous frame it should sample from, applies a decay factor, and outputs the result.

5.6.2 Chromatic Aberration

One of the most visually striking effects unique to the GLSL approach is chromatic aberration — separating the red, green, and blue channels and transforming each one slightly differently. In physical optics, chromatic aberration occurs because different wavelengths of light refract by different amounts through a lens. 5.7 What Is a Signed Distance Function?

We now turn to the second major topic of this chapter: signed distance functions (SDFs). If frame feedback is about building complexity through time, SDFs are about building complexity through space — describing shapes not by their outlines or vertices, but by a single mathematical function that encodes the entire geometry.

📖 Definition

Signed Distance Function (SDF) — A function $f(p)$ that, given any point $p$ in space, returns the shortest distance from that point to the surface (boundary) of a shape. The “signed” part means the function returns:

  • Positive values when the point is outside the shape
  • Negative values when the point is inside the shape
  • Zero when the point is exactly on the boundary

Why are SDFs so useful? Consider the alternatives for describing a circle of radius 0.3 centered at the origin. In an imperative drawing system, you might call circle(0, 0, 0.3) — a command that produces pixels but gives you no way to query the shape mathematically. In a polygon-based system, you might approximate the circle with 64 line segments — a representation that loses the shape’s perfect smoothness. With an SDF, you write:

$$f(p) = |p| - 0.3$$

This single expression encodes everything about the circle. Points where $|p| < 0.3$ are inside (negative). Points where $|p| > 0.3$ are outside (positive). Points where $|p| = 0.3$ are on the boundary (zero). And the absolute value of $f(p)$ tells you exactly how far you are from the nearest point on the circle — information that is extraordinarily useful for rendering effects like anti-aliased edges, glow, outlines, and shadows.

SDFs are the native language of shader-based rendering. Because a fragment shader runs independently for every pixel, and an SDF can be evaluated independently for every point, the two are a natural match. Each pixel evaluates the SDF at its coordinates and uses the distance value to determine its color. There is no need for geometry buffers, vertex lists, or rasterization — the shape exists purely as a mathematical function.

📜 Historical Note

Inigo Quilez is the most important figure in the practical development of SDFs for real-time graphics. A Spanish mathematician and software engineer who co-created ShaderToy, Quilez compiled an extensive catalog of 2D and 3D SDF primitives at iquilezles.org that has become the definitive reference for shader artists worldwide. His work demonstrated that SDFs, combined with a technique called ray marching, could render complex 3D scenes entirely within a fragment shader — with no geometry, no meshes, and no traditional 3D pipeline. Nearly every SDF formula used in this chapter comes from or is inspired by Quilez’s work.

5.8 SDF Primitives

Just as complex drawings are built from simple shapes, complex SDFs are built from simple primitives — the basic building blocks of signed distance geometry. Let us examine the most common 2D SDF primitives, understand how each formula works, and see them rendered in a shader.

5.8.1 Circle

The simplest and most fundamental SDF is the circle. A circle centered at the origin with radius $r$:

$$f(p) = |p| - r$$

float sdCircle(vec2 p, float r) {
  return length(p) - r;
}

The logic is transparent: length(p) gives the distance from point $p$ to the origin. Subtracting the radius shifts the zero-crossing to the circle’s boundary. Points inside the circle have $|p| < r$, so the result is negative. Points outside have $|p| > r$, so the result is positive.

To move the circle to a different center position $c$, simply subtract the center before evaluating: sdCircle(p - c, r). This “translate by subtraction” pattern works for all SDFs.

5.8.2 Box (Rectangle)

A 2D box centered at the origin with half-dimensions $b = (b_x, b_y)$:

$$f(p) = |\max(|p| - b, 0)| + \min(\max(|p|_x - b_x,\; |p|_y - b_y),\; 0)$$

float sdBox(vec2 p, vec2 b) {
  vec2 d = abs(p) - b;
  return length(max(d, 0.0)) + min(max(d.x, d.y), 0.0);
}

This formula, from Inigo Quilez, is more complex than the circle but handles all cases correctly:

  • abs(p) exploits the box’s four-fold symmetry — we only need to consider one quadrant
  • abs(p) - b gives the signed distance in each axis independently
  • length(max(d, 0.0)) handles the exterior — the Euclidean distance from the nearest corner or edge
  • min(max(d.x, d.y), 0.0) handles the interior — the (negative) distance to the nearest edge from inside

5.8.3 Line Segment

A line segment from point $a$ to point $b$:

float sdSegment(vec2 p, vec2 a, vec2 b) {
  vec2 pa = p - a;
  vec2 ba = b - a;
  float h = clamp(dot(pa, ba) / dot(ba, ba), 0.0, 1.0);
  return length(pa - ba * h);
}

The dot(pa, ba) / dot(ba, ba) projects point $p$ onto the line defined by $a$ and $b$. The clamp restricts this projection to the segment endpoints, so points beyond the ends measure distance to the nearest endpoint rather than the infinite line.

5.8.4 Ring (Annulus)

A ring — the region between two concentric circles — has a beautifully simple SDF. Given an outer radius $R$ and a thickness $t$:

float sdRing(vec2 p, float R, float t) {
  return abs(length(p) - R) - t;
}

The inner expression length(p) - R gives the SDF of a circle at radius $R$. Taking the abs() of that turns the signed distance into an unsigned distance from the circle — it is zero on the circle and positive everywhere else. Subtracting the thickness $t$ then “inflates” that circle into a ring of width $2t$.

5.9 Rendering with SDFs

Having an SDF gives you a distance value for every pixel, but you still need to convert that distance into a visible color. The way you do this conversion determines the visual style of your rendering. SDFs support a wide range of rendering techniques, from sharp geometric shapes to soft glowing forms.

5.9.1 Sharp Edges with step()

The simplest rendering: color the pixel white if the distance is negative (inside the shape) and black if positive (outside):

vec3 col = d < 0.0 ? vec3(1.0) : vec3(0.0);
// Or equivalently:
vec3 col = vec3(1.0 - step(0.0, d));

This produces a perfectly sharp boundary with no anti-aliasing. On high-resolution screens the result looks fine, but on standard displays the edges will appear jagged (aliased).

5.9.2 Anti-aliased Edges with smoothstep()

The workhorse of SDF rendering is smoothstep(), which creates a smooth transition over a small distance range:

vec3 col = vec3(1.0 - smoothstep(0.0, 0.005, d));

The transition zone (from 0.0 to 0.005 in this example) should be approximately one pixel wide in UV space. For a 400-pixel canvas with UV coordinates ranging roughly from −0.5 to 0.5, one pixel is about $1/400 = 0.0025$, so a smoothstep width of 0.003 to 0.005 gives a pleasantly anti-aliased edge.

5.9.3 Contour Lines

Contour lines visualize the distance field as a topographic map. Apply a periodic function to the distance value:

// Contour lines using cos
col *= 0.8 + 0.2 * cos(100.0 * d);

// Contour lines using fract
float contour = fract(d * 20.0);
col *= smoothstep(0.0, 0.05, contour) * smoothstep(1.0, 0.95, contour);

The cos approach creates soft, sinusoidal undulations. The fract approach creates sharper bands. Both reveal the structure of the distance field, showing how distance varies smoothly across the entire image.

5.9.4 Glow Effects

SDFs make glow effects trivial. The glow intensity is simply the inverse of the distance — bright near the shape, fading with distance:

// Simple glow
vec3 glow = vec3(0.02 / abs(d));

// Colored glow with falloff control
vec3 glow = vec3(1.0, 0.5, 0.2) * (0.01 / (abs(d) + 0.001));

The + 0.001 prevents division by zero exactly on the boundary. The glow falls off as $1/d$, which is physically motivated (light intensity decreases with distance from the source).

GLSL SDF Rendering Techniques

This shader demonstrates four rendering layers simultaneously: a filled interior, contour lines, a sharp white border, and an outer glow. All four derive from the same distance value $d$. This is the power of SDFs — one distance function supports unlimited rendering effects.

5.10 Boolean Operations on SDFs

One of the most powerful aspects of signed distance functions is how naturally they compose. You can combine two SDFs using simple arithmetic to create union, intersection, and subtraction — the same boolean operations used in constructive solid geometry (CSG), but implemented with a single line of code.

5.10.1 Union (OR)

The union of two shapes includes all points that are inside either shape. In SDF terms, a point is inside the union if its distance to either shape is negative. The combined SDF is simply the minimum of the two distances:

float opUnion(float d1, float d2) {
  return min(d1, d2);
}

This works because the minimum selects the closest shape boundary. If a point is inside shape A (distance negative) and outside shape B (distance positive), the minimum picks the negative value, correctly indicating the point is inside the union.

5.10.2 Intersection (AND)

The intersection includes only points that are inside both shapes. The combined SDF is the maximum:

float opIntersection(float d1, float d2) {
  return max(d1, d2);
}

A point is inside both shapes only if both distances are negative. The maximum of two negative numbers is the less negative one — which is correct, since the intersection boundary is the farthest boundary from the deepest interior.

5.10.3 Subtraction (NOT)

Subtraction removes one shape from another. To subtract shape B from shape A, negate B’s SDF and take the maximum with A:

float opSubtraction(float d1, float d2) {
  return max(d1, -d2);
}

Negating d2 flips the inside and outside of shape B. Taking the maximum with d1 then keeps only the region that is inside A but outside B.

5.10.4 Smooth Union

The boolean operations above produce sharp creases where shapes meet. For organic, blobby shapes, we want a smooth blend. The smooth minimum (or “smooth union”) function, also from Inigo Quilez, creates a smooth transition:

float opSmoothUnion(float d1, float d2, float k) {
  float h = clamp(0.5 + 0.5 * (d2 - d1) / k, 0.0, 1.0);
  return mix(d2, d1, h) - k * h * (1.0 - h);
}

The parameter $k$ controls the smoothness of the blend. Larger values produce a more gradual, blobby merge; smaller values approach the sharp union. A value of $k = 0.1$ produces a noticeable but subtle blend; $k = 0.3$ creates very organic, melted-together shapes.

Try changing opSmoothUnion to min (sharp union), max (intersection), or max(box, -circle) (subtraction) to see the other boolean operations in action.

5.11 Morphing Between Shapes

Because SDFs are just numbers — continuous, smooth scalar values — you can interpolate between them to create seamless shape morphing. The GLSL mix() function performs linear interpolation: mix(a, b, t) returns $a \cdot (1 - t) + b \cdot t$. When applied to two SDF values, it smoothly transitions from one shape to another as $t$ goes from 0 to 1.

$$d_{\text{morph}} = (1 - t) \cdot d_1 + t \cdot d_2$$

When $t = 0$, the result is purely shape 1. When $t = 1$, it is purely shape 2. At $t = 0.5$, it is a 50/50 blend — a form that is half-circle, half-box, or whatever your two primitives are. The intermediate shapes are mathematically meaningful distance fields, not just pixel-level crossfades, so edges remain sharp and the rendering techniques from Section 5.9 (glow, contour lines, anti-aliasing) work perfectly throughout the morph.

💡 Key Concept

SDF morphing vs. pixel morphing. A pixel-level crossfade between two images blends their colors: at $t = 0.5$, every pixel is a ghostly 50% overlay. SDF morphing blends the geometry: at $t = 0.5$, you get a crisp shape that is geometrically halfway between the two originals. The boundary of the intermediate shape is well-defined and smooth, because the interpolated SDF is itself a valid distance field (approximately). This is a fundamentally different and more powerful form of interpolation.

To create an animated morph, drive $t$ with a time-based oscillation:

float t = 0.5 + 0.5 * sin(u_time);  // oscillates 0 to 1
float d = mix(sdCircle(uv, 0.2), sdBox(uv, vec2(0.15)), t);

These intermediate forms are not pre-designed; they emerge from the mathematics of linear interpolation applied to distance fields.

5.12 Combining Feedback with SDFs

We have now mastered both frame feedback and signed distance functions independently. The approach is straightforward: use the five-step feedback loop from Section 5.4, and in step 3 (draw new content), render an SDF shape using the techniques from Sections 5.8–5.10. The SDF gives you precise, anti-aliased shapes with glow and contour effects. The feedback accumulates and transforms them over time. ✍ Knowledge Check

When combining SDF rendering with frame feedback, at which step of the five-step feedback loop do you draw the SDF shapes?

New content — including SDF shapes — is drawn in step 3 of the feedback loop, after the previous frame has been drawn back with a transformation (step 2). This new content is then captured by the framebuffer and will be part of the feedback transformation on the next frame.

Chapter 5 Review Quiz

Test your understanding of frame feedback and signed distance functions.

✍ Question 1

In a frame feedback system, what happens if the tint alpha is set to exactly 255 (no decay)?

With no decay (alpha = 255), pixel values never decrease. Each frame adds new content without removing old content, and the slight scale/rotation transformation cannot reduce brightness. Over time, the image saturates to solid white. A decay value slightly below 255 (such as 248–253) is essential for maintaining visual balance.

✍ Question 2

What does a signed distance function return for a point that is inside the shape?

An SDF returns a negative value for points inside the shape, a positive value for points outside, and zero for points exactly on the boundary. The magnitude (absolute value) is the distance to the nearest point on the boundary.

✍ Question 3

Which SDF combination operation uses min(d1, d2)?

Union uses min() because a point is inside the union if it is inside either shape — that is, if the minimum of the two distances is negative. Intersection uses max(), subtraction uses max(d1, -d2), and smooth union uses the polynomial smooth minimum formula.

✍ Question 4

In GLSL, mat2(1, 2, 3, 4) creates a matrix. Which element is in row 1, column 2?

GLSL fills matrices column by column. So mat2(1, 2, 3, 4) creates columns $(1, 2)$ and $(3, 4)$, yielding the matrix $\begin{pmatrix}1 & 3 \\ 2 & 4\end{pmatrix}$. Row 1, column 2 (using 1-based indexing) is the element 3.

✍ Question 5

Who are the pioneering video artists most closely associated with analog video feedback in the 1960s–70s?

Nam June Paik is widely regarded as the founder of video art, and the Vasulkas (Steina and Woody) made video feedback central to their practice. Both worked extensively with the feedback loop created by pointing a camera at a monitor displaying its own output. Quilez and Perlin are graphics researchers; Reas and Fry created Processing; Molnár and Nake were early computer art pioneers.
