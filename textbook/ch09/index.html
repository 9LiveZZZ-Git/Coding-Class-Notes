    9.1 Calculus for Simulation

    Calculus is the mathematics of change. It was invented independently by Isaac Newton and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz in the late 17th century, and it remains the most powerful tool we have for describing how quantities evolve over time. For simulation, two ideas from calculus are essential: derivatives (rates of change) and integrals (accumulation of change).

    📜 Historical Note

    Isaac Newton developed calculus ("the method of fluxions") in the 1660s to solve problems in mechanics and gravitation. Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz independently invented calculus around 1675 and introduced much of the notation we still use ($dx$, $\int$, $dy/dx$). Their bitter priority dispute lasted decades, but both deserve credit for one of the greatest intellectual achievements in human history.

    9.1.1 Derivatives: Rates of Change

    A derivative measures how fast a quantity is changing at a given instant. If you are driving a car and your position along the road is $x(t)$, then your velocity is:

    $$v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{x(t + \Delta t) - x(t)}{\Delta t}$$

    This says: take two positions very close together in time, compute how far you moved, divide by the time interval, and let that interval shrink to zero. The result is your instantaneous velocity — how fast your position is changing right now.

    Think of a speedometer. At any given instant, it reads your speed — the rate at which your position is changing. That speedometer reading is the derivative of your position. You do not need to drive for a full hour to have a speed at this instant; the derivative captures the instantaneous rate, even though the actual computation involves a limit of finite differences.

    Some important derivatives you will encounter in physics:

    • $\frac{d}{dt}(at + b) = a$ — constant velocity gives zero acceleration
    • $\frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\frac{1}{2}at^2\right) = at$ — parabolic motion gives linear velocity
    • $\frac{d}{dt}\sin(\omega t) = \omega\cos(\omega t)$ — oscillatory motion
    📖 Definition

    Derivative — The derivative $\frac{dx}{dt}$ of a function $x(t)$ measures the instantaneous rate of change of $x$ with respect to $t$. Geometrically, it is the slope of the tangent line to the graph of $x(t)$ at time $t$.

    In physics, derivatives form a chain:

    $$\text{Position } x(t) \xrightarrow{\frac{d}{dt}} \text{Velocity } v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} \xrightarrow{\frac{d}{dt}} \text{Acceleration } a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}$$

    Velocity is the derivative of position (how fast position changes). Acceleration is the derivative of velocity (how fast velocity changes). Acceleration is also the second derivative of position. In our simulations, forces determine acceleration through Newton's second law, and from acceleration we reconstruct velocity and position.

    9.1.2 Integrals: Accumulation

    An integral is the reverse of a derivative. If a derivative breaks a quantity into its rate of change, an integral accumulates those rates of change back into the original quantity.

    $$x(t) = x(0) + \int_0^t v(\tau)\, d\tau$$

    This says: to find the position at time $t$, start with the initial position $x(0)$ and add up all the tiny displacements $v(\tau)\,d\tau$ from time $0$ to time $t$. Geometrically, the integral is the area under the curve of the velocity function.

    📖 Definition

    Integral — The definite integral $\int_a^b f(t)\,dt$ computes the accumulated total of $f(t)$ over the interval $[a, b]$. It represents the signed area between the curve and the horizontal axis.

    9.1.3 The Fundamental Theorem (Intuitive)

    📐 Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Informal)

    Differentiation and integration are inverse operations. If you differentiate a function and then integrate the result, you get back the original function (up to a constant). Conversely, if you integrate a function and then differentiate, you recover the original function.

    $$\frac{d}{dt}\int_0^t f(\tau)\,d\tau = f(t)$$

    For simulation this means: we know forces (which give us acceleration), and we need positions. We integrate acceleration to get velocity, then integrate velocity to get position. Each frame of our simulation performs one small step of this integration: 

    // These two lines ARE numerical integration:
this.velocity.add(this.acceleration);   // Integrate acceleration -> velocity
this.position.add(this.velocity);       // Integrate velocity -> position

    Each frame, we add one frame's worth of acceleration to velocity (a tiny integral step), then add one frame's worth of velocity to position (another tiny integral step). Over many frames, these tiny additions accumulate into the full motion.

    9.1.4 The Connection: Position, Velocity, Acceleration

    These concepts form an inseparable chain through calculus:

    Going down (differentiation):

    $$\text{Position } x(t) \xrightarrow{\frac{d}{dt}} \text{Velocity } v(t) \xrightarrow{\frac{d}{dt}} \text{Acceleration } a(t)$$

    Going up (integration):

    $$\text{Acceleration } a(t) \xrightarrow{\int dt} \text{Velocity } v(t) \xrightarrow{\int dt} \text{Position } x(t)$$

    In simulation, we always go "up": we know the forces (which give us acceleration via $F = ma$), and we integrate twice to get position. Each frame of our simulation loop performs two small integration steps: one to update velocity from acceleration, and one to update position from velocity.

    Here is a concrete example. Suppose an object starts at position $x = 0$ with velocity $v = 10$ and experiences constant acceleration $a = -2$ (deceleration). The exact solution is:

    $$v(t) = 10 - 2t, \quad x(t) = 10t - t^2$$

    The object moves forward, slowing down, reaches maximum displacement at $t = 5$, then reverses. Our simulation will approximate this trajectory frame by frame. The better our integration method, the closer our discrete trajectory will match this exact parabola.

    9.1.5 Discrete Time vs. Continuous Time

    In real physics, time flows continuously. In a simulation, time advances in discrete steps called frames or time steps. Each frame is a snapshot. We approximate the continuous flow by taking small steps:

    $$v(t + \Delta t) \approx v(t) + a(t) \cdot \Delta t$$

    $$x(t + \Delta t) \approx x(t) + v(t) \cdot \Delta t$$

    The smaller $\Delta t$ is, the more accurate the approximation. At 60 frames per second with $\Delta t = 1/60 \approx 0.0167$ seconds, most artistic simulations look smooth and behave correctly. But the choice of how we compute these updates — the integration method — matters enormously, as we will see in Section 9.3.

    A useful analogy: imagine estimating the area of a lake on a map by covering it with small squares. Smaller squares give a better approximation. Integration is the same idea applied to curves over time. Our time step $\Delta t$ is the width of each "square." Smaller steps give more accuracy but require more computation.

    💡 Key Concept

    The simulation loop is a chain: Forces $\xrightarrow{F=ma}$ Acceleration $\xrightarrow{\text{integrate}}$ Velocity $\xrightarrow{\text{integrate}}$ Position. This chain is the heart of every physics simulation.

    9.2 Ordinary Differential Equations

    Before we study integration methods and force laws in detail, we need to understand the mathematical framework that unifies all of our simulations: ordinary differential equations.

    📐 Newton's Three Laws of Motion

    First Law (Inertia): An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in uniform motion, unless acted upon by a net external force.

    Second Law: $\vec{F} = m\vec{a}$. The net force on an object equals its mass times its acceleration. Equivalently, $\vec{a} = \vec{F}/m$.

    Third Law: For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. If body A exerts force $\vec{F}$ on body B, then body B exerts force $-\vec{F}$ on body A.

    Newton's Second Law is the bridge between forces and motion. It tells us that if we know all the forces acting on an object, we can compute its acceleration. And from acceleration, through integration, we get velocity and position. The entire structure of our simulations rests on this single equation.

    In code, the principle of superposition says that all forces add together: 

    // Multiple forces can be applied per frame; they add up
applyForce(force) {
  let f = p5.Vector.div(force, this.mass); // a = F/m
  this.acceleration.add(f);  // Accumulate contributions
}

    9.2.1 What Is an ODE?

    An Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) is an equation that relates a function to its derivatives. The word "ordinary" means it involves derivatives with respect to a single variable (usually time), as opposed to "partial" differential equations which involve multiple variables (like space and time).

    📖 Definition

    Ordinary Differential Equation — An equation of the form $\frac{dx}{dt} = f(x, t)$ that describes how the state variable $x$ changes over time. The function $f$ on the right-hand side is called the vector field — it tells us the rate of change at every point in state space.

    Every simulation we build is an ODE system. The simplest example is a falling ball:

    $$\frac{dx}{dt} = v, \quad \frac{dv}{dt} = -g$$

    This says: position changes at the rate of velocity, and velocity changes at the rate of (negative) gravitational acceleration. Given initial conditions $x(0) = x_0$ and $v(0) = v_0$, the solution is the familiar parabolic trajectory.

    9.2.2 First-Order vs. Higher-Order

    The order of an ODE is the highest derivative that appears. A first-order ODE involves only the first derivative:

    $$\frac{dx}{dt} = f(x, t)$$

    A second-order ODE involves the second derivative. Newton's second law is a second-order ODE:

    $$m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = F(x, v, t)$$

    This is equivalent to $ma = F$, since acceleration $a = \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}$.

    9.2.3 Converting to a System of First-Order ODEs

    Any higher-order ODE can be converted into a system of first-order ODEs by introducing new variables. For Newton's second law:

    Let $v = \frac{dx}{dt}$. Then:

    $$\frac{dx}{dt} = v$$

    $$\frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{F(x, v, t)}{m}$$

    Now we have two first-order equations instead of one second-order equation. This is exactly what our simulation code does: we track both position and velocity as separate state variables, and update each one according to its own first-order equation.

    💡 Key Concept

    Initial Value Problem (IVP) — An ODE together with initial conditions (starting values for all state variables) defines an initial value problem. Given the equations and starting state, the solution is a unique trajectory through state space. Our simulations solve IVPs numerically, frame by frame.

    9.2.4 Example: The Simple Harmonic Oscillator

    The simplest physically interesting ODE is the simple harmonic oscillator (an ideal spring with no damping):

    $$m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = -kx$$

    Converting to a first-order system:

    $$\frac{dx}{dt} = v, \quad \frac{dv}{dt} = -\frac{k}{m}x$$

    The exact solution is $x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$ where $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$ is the angular frequency and $A$ and $\phi$ are determined by initial conditions. The motion is periodic: the mass oscillates back and forth forever with constant amplitude. This is the reference behavior we use to test integration methods — if Forward Euler makes the amplitude grow, we know the method is adding energy.

    9.2.5 Beyond Physics: ODE Systems Everywhere

    ODEs model far more than bouncing balls. They model epidemics (SIR model), weather (Lorenz attractor), population dynamics (Lotka-Volterra), chemical reactions, neural activity, and countless other phenomena. The simulation technique is always the same:

    1. Identify the state variables ($x$, $y$, $z$, etc.)
    2. Write the equations: $\frac{dx}{dt} = \ldots$, $\frac{dy}{dt} = \ldots$
    3. Choose parameter values and initial conditions
    4. Each frame: compute the right-hand sides, multiply by $\Delta t$, add to state

    The general recipe in code:

    // General ODE integration (Euler method):
let dx = f(x, y, z) * dt;
let dy = g(x, y, z) * dt;
let dz = h(x, y, z) * dt;

x += dx;
y += dy;
z += dz;

    This pattern is exactly the same whether we are simulating a spring, an epidemic, a chemical reaction, or a strange attractor. The only thing that changes is the right-hand side functions $f$, $g$, $h$. This universality is what makes ODEs so powerful.

    9.3 Numerical Integration Methods

    The choice of integration method determines whether your simulation is stable, accurate, and energy-conserving. A bad method can make a spring oscillation grow without bound or an orbit spiral outward. Let us examine the most important methods.

    9.3.1 Forward (Explicit) Euler

    The simplest method. It uses the current state to predict the next state:

    $$x_{n+1} = x_n + v_n \cdot \Delta t$$

    $$v_{n+1} = v_n + a_n \cdot \Delta t$$

    Both updates use values from time step $n$ only. This is straightforward but problematic: Forward Euler systematically adds energy to oscillatory systems. A spring that should oscillate forever will instead grow in amplitude until the simulation explodes.

    ⚠ Common Mistake

    Forward Euler looks correct but adds energy to spring/pendulum systems over time. For artistic simulations that run indefinitely, this energy drift will eventually cause the simulation to blow up.

    9.3.2 Semi-Implicit (Symplectic) Euler

    One tiny change makes an enormous difference: update velocity first, then use the updated velocity to update position:

    $$v_{n+1} = v_n + a_n \cdot \Delta t$$

    $$x_{n+1} = x_n + v_{n+1} \cdot \Delta t$$

    The only difference is that the position update uses $v_{n+1}$ instead of $v_n$. This method is called symplectic because it preserves the geometric structure of Hamiltonian systems. In practice, it approximately conserves energy: oscillations remain stable indefinitely.

    📐 Semi-Implicit Euler Method

    Update velocity first, then use the new velocity to update position:

    $$v_{n+1} = v_n + a(x_n) \cdot \Delta t$$

    $$x_{n+1} = x_n + v_{n+1} \cdot \Delta t$$

    This is the recommended integration method for real-time physics simulations. It is first-order accurate but energy-stable.

    9.3.3 Verlet Integration

    Verlet integration computes position directly from the previous two positions, bypassing velocity entirely:

    $$x_{n+1} = 2x_n - x_{n-1} + a_n \cdot \Delta t^2$$

    Velocity can be recovered when needed: $v_n \approx \frac{x_{n+1} - x_{n-1}}{2\Delta t}$. Verlet integration is second-order accurate (error proportional to $\Delta t^2$) and symplectic, making it excellent for molecular dynamics and cloth simulation. It is also naturally stable for constraints (like fixed-length rods).

    The Verlet method was discovered by the French physicist Loup Verlet in 1967 for molecular dynamics simulations, though the same formula was known to Stormer (1907) and even Newton himself used a similar approach. Its simplicity, accuracy, and energy-conservation properties make it the standard for molecular dynamics and constraint-based simulations.

    In code, Verlet integration looks like this:

    // Verlet integration (stores previous position instead of velocity)
let temp = pos.copy();
pos.x = 2 * pos.x - prevPos.x + acc.x * dt * dt;
pos.y = 2 * pos.y - prevPos.y + acc.y * dt * dt;
prevPos = temp;

// Recover velocity when needed:
// vel = (pos - prevPos) / (2 * dt)
    💡 Key Concept

    Verlet's advantage for constraints: Because Verlet uses positions directly (not velocities), it is easy to enforce constraints like fixed-length connections. Simply move the positions to satisfy the constraint after each step. This is the basis of Position-Based Dynamics, widely used in game engines for cloth, ropes, and ragdolls.

    9.3.4 Runge-Kutta (RK4)

    The fourth-order Runge-Kutta method is the gold standard for accuracy. It evaluates the derivative at four points within each time step and combines them:

    $$k_1 = f(t_n, y_n)$$

    $$k_2 = f\!\left(t_n + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2},\; y_n + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\,k_1\right)$$

    $$k_3 = f\!\left(t_n + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2},\; y_n + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\,k_2\right)$$

    $$k_4 = f(t_n + \Delta t,\; y_n + \Delta t\,k_3)$$

    $$y_{n+1} = y_n + \frac{\Delta t}{6}(k_1 + 2k_2 + 2k_3 + k_4)$$

    RK4 is fourth-order accurate (error proportional to $\Delta t^4$), which means it can take much larger time steps while maintaining accuracy. However, it is not symplectic and does not conserve energy in long simulations. It is best for short simulations or systems where accuracy matters more than energy conservation (e.g., computing the Lorenz attractor).

    The intuition behind RK4 is similar to Simpson's rule for numerical integration: instead of evaluating the slope only at the current point (like Euler), we sample the slope at four places within the time step and take a weighted average. The midpoint evaluations ($k_2$ and $k_3$) get double weight, similar to how trapezoidal and Simpson's rules improve upon left-endpoint Riemann sums.

    Here is a simplified RK4 implementation for a 1D system $\frac{dy}{dt} = f(t, y)$:

    function rk4Step(t, y, dt, f) {
  let k1 = f(t, y);
  let k2 = f(t + dt/2, y + dt/2 * k1);
  let k3 = f(t + dt/2, y + dt/2 * k2);
  let k4 = f(t + dt, y + dt * k3);
  return y + (dt / 6) * (k1 + 2*k2 + 2*k3 + k4);
}
    📜 Historical Note

    The Runge-Kutta methods were developed by the German mathematicians Carl Runge (1895) and Martin Kutta (1901). The fourth-order variant (RK4) became the workhorse of scientific computing for nearly a century, until symplectic methods became preferred for long-time integrations in the late 20th century.

    What is the key difference between Forward Euler and Semi-Implicit Euler integration?

    Semi-Implicit (Symplectic) Euler first computes $v_{n+1} = v_n + a \Delta t$, then uses this updated velocity to compute $x_{n+1} = x_n + v_{n+1} \Delta t$.     9.4 Spring Forces

    9.4.1 Hooke's Law

    In 1660, Robert Hooke discovered that the force exerted by an elastic material is proportional to how far it is deformed from its natural length. This relationship, known as Hooke's law, is one of the most useful force models in simulation.

    📐 Hooke's Law

    $$F = -k \cdot x$$

    where $k$ is the spring constant (stiffness), $x$ is the displacement from rest length, and the negative sign indicates the force is restoring — always directed back toward the equilibrium position.

    A stiffer spring (larger $k$) exerts more force for the same displacement. The angular frequency of oscillation is $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$, and the period is $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$. Heavier objects oscillate more slowly; stiffer springs oscillate faster.

    📜 Historical Note

    Hooke originally published his law as an anagram: "ceiiinosssttuv," which rearranges to the Latin phrase "ut tensio, sic vis" ("as the extension, so the force"). He did this to establish priority while keeping the discovery secret. Hooke's rivalry with Newton is one of the most famous feuds in the history of science.

    In 2D or 3D, we need the vector form of Hooke's law. Given an anchor point and a bob connected by a spring: 

    function springForceVec(anchor, bob, restLen, k) {
  let spring = p5.Vector.sub(bob, anchor);
  let currentLen = spring.mag();
  let displacement = currentLen - restLen;
  spring.normalize();
  spring.mult(-k * displacement);
  return spring; // Force on the bob
}

    The force direction is along the spring axis. When the spring is stretched ($\text{currentLen} > \text{restLen}$), the displacement is positive and the force pulls the bob back toward the anchor. When compressed, the displacement is negative and the force pushes the bob away.

    The potential energy stored in a spring is $U = \frac{1}{2}k x^2$. This energy is zero at the rest length and increases quadratically as the spring is stretched or compressed. The kinetic energy of the bob is $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. In an ideal (undamped) spring system, total energy $E = K + U$ is constant: energy flows back and forth between kinetic and potential forms as the spring oscillates.

    9.4.2 Damped Springs

    Real springs lose energy to friction, air resistance, and internal deformation. We model this with a damping force proportional to velocity and opposing the motion:

    $$F_{\text{total}} = -k \cdot x - b \cdot v$$

    where $b$ is the damping coefficient. The damping term $-bv$ always opposes velocity, removing kinetic energy from the system. Three regimes exist:

    • Underdamped ($b < 2\sqrt{km}$): oscillates with decaying amplitude
    • Critically damped ($b = 2\sqrt{km}$): returns to equilibrium as fast as possible without oscillating
    • Overdamped ($b > 2\sqrt{km}$): returns slowly without oscillating

    9.4.3 Spring-Mass Systems and Cloth

    A single spring is interesting. A network of springs is powerful. By connecting masses with springs in a grid, we can simulate cloth, jelly, soft bodies, and bridges. Each mass point feels forces from all springs attached to it, plus gravity and damping.

    For a cloth simulation, we typically use three types of springs:

    • Structural springs: connect immediate horizontal and vertical neighbors (maintain shape)
    • Shear springs: connect diagonal neighbors (resist shearing)
    • Bend springs: connect every other node (resist folding)

    9.5 Gravity and Orbital Mechanics

    9.5.1 Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation

    📐 Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation

    Every object with mass attracts every other object with mass. The force is:

    $$F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$$

    where $G$ is the gravitational constant, $m_1$ and $m_2$ are the masses, and $r$ is the distance between their centers. The force is always attractive, directed along the line connecting the two objects.

    This is an inverse-square law: the force drops off as the square of the distance. At twice the distance, the force is $1/4$ as strong. At ten times the distance, $1/100$. This creates rich dynamics: nearby objects interact strongly, while distant ones barely influence each other.

    In code:

    function gravitationalForce(bodyA, bodyB) {
  let force = p5.Vector.sub(bodyB.position, bodyA.position);
  let distance = constrain(force.mag(), 20, 500);
  let G = 0.5;
  let strength = (G * bodyA.mass * bodyB.mass) / (distance * distance);
  force.setMag(strength);
  return force; // pulls bodyA toward bodyB
}
    ⚠ Common Mistake

    Always clamp the minimum distance! When two bodies get very close, $r \to 0$ and $F \to \infty$, causing the simulation to explode. Use constrain(distance, minDist, maxDist) to prevent singularities.

    9.5.2 The Two-Body Problem and Orbits

    When one body orbits another, the gravitational force provides the centripetal acceleration needed for circular (or elliptical) motion. For a stable circular orbit, the initial velocity must be perpendicular to the line connecting the bodies and have the right magnitude:

    $$v_{\text{orbit}} = \sqrt{\frac{G \cdot M}{r}}$$

    Too slow and the body falls inward. Too fast and it escapes. Just right and it orbits in an ellipse (or circle).

    9.5.3 Kepler's Laws (Conceptual)

    Johannes Kepler discovered three laws of planetary motion empirically, before Newton explained them with gravity:

    1. Law of Orbits: Planets move in ellipses with the sun at one focus.
    2. Law of Areas: A line from the sun to a planet sweeps equal areas in equal times. (Planets move faster when closer to the sun.)
    3. Law of Periods: The square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis: $T^2 \propto a^3$.

    Our simulations naturally reproduce Kepler's laws when we use an inverse-square gravitational force with a symplectic integrator. This is a beautiful validation: the emergent behavior matches centuries of astronomical observation.

    9.5.4 N-Body Simulation

    With more than two bodies, there is no analytical solution. We must simulate numerically. For $N$ bodies, we compute all $\frac{N(N-1)}{2}$ pairwise interactions each frame.

    According to Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation, if the distance between two objects doubles, what happens to the gravitational force?

    Gravity follows an inverse-square law: $F \propto 1/r^2$. If $r$ doubles, $F$ becomes $1/(2^2) = 1/4$ of its original value. This rapid falloff is why nearby objects dominate gravitational interactions.

    9.6 Drag and Friction

    9.6.1 Friction (Coulomb Friction)

    Kinetic friction acts on objects sliding along a surface. It has a constant magnitude (independent of speed) and always opposes the direction of motion:

    $$\vec{F}_{\text{friction}} = -\mu \cdot N \cdot \hat{v}$$

    where $\mu$ is the coefficient of friction, $N$ is the normal force (how hard the surfaces are pressed together), and $\hat{v}$ is the unit vector in the direction of velocity. On a flat surface, $N = mg$.

    9.6.2 Linear Drag

    For slow-moving objects (low Reynolds number), drag is proportional to velocity:

    $$\vec{F}_{\text{drag}} = -c_1 \cdot \vec{v}$$

    This is sometimes called viscous drag or Stokes drag, and models objects moving through thick fluids like honey.

    9.6.3 Quadratic Drag (Air Resistance)

    For everyday objects moving through air (high Reynolds number), drag is proportional to the square of speed:

    $$\vec{F}_{\text{drag}} = -c_2 \cdot |\vec{v}|^2 \cdot \hat{v}$$

    The quadratic dependence on speed means drag grows very quickly. At low speeds, drag is negligible. At high speeds, it dominates all other forces.

    9.6.4 Friction vs. Drag: A Comparison

    It is important to understand when each model applies:

    • Friction is constant regardless of speed. A slow object and a fast object on the same surface feel the same friction force. It is most relevant for objects sliding on surfaces.
    • Linear drag ($F \propto v$) applies at low speeds in viscous fluids. Think of a marble sinking slowly through honey.
    • Quadratic drag ($F \propto v^2$) applies at everyday speeds in air and water. It grows rapidly: double the speed means four times the drag force.

    In most artistic simulations, linear drag (velocity damping) is the simplest and most common choice. It is easy to implement and creates smooth, natural-looking deceleration: 

    // Simple velocity damping (linear drag)
this.velocity.mult(0.99); // Remove 1% of velocity each frame

    9.6.5 Terminal Velocity

    When a falling object's drag force equals the gravitational force, acceleration becomes zero and the object reaches terminal velocity:

    $$mg = c_2 \cdot v_t^2 \implies v_t = \sqrt{\frac{mg}{c_2}}$$

    Heavier objects have higher terminal velocities. Objects with large cross-sections (like parachutes) have lower terminal velocities because $c_2$ is larger.

    💡 Key Concept

    A skydiver has a terminal velocity of about 55 m/s (120 mph) in a spread-eagle position. With a parachute deployed, terminal velocity drops to about 5 m/s (11 mph) — an 11-fold decrease — because the parachute's large area dramatically increases the drag coefficient $c_2$.

    9.7 Constraints and Collisions

    9.7.1 Boundary Reflection

    The simplest collision is bouncing off a wall. When an object crosses a boundary, we reflect its position and negate the velocity component perpendicular to the wall: 

    // Bounce off floor
if (pos.y > height) {
  pos.y = height;
  vel.y *= -restitution; // restitution: 1.0 = perfectly elastic, 0 = no bounce
}

    9.7.2 Elastic vs. Inelastic Collisions

    When two objects collide, momentum is always conserved. Whether kinetic energy is also conserved determines the type of collision:

    📖 Definition

    Elastic collision — both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved. The objects bounce off each other perfectly.

    Inelastic collision — momentum is conserved but kinetic energy is not. Some energy is lost to deformation, heat, or sound.

    9.7.3 Conservation of Momentum

    📐 Conservation of Momentum

    $$m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = m_1 v_1' + m_2 v_2'$$

    The total momentum before the collision equals the total momentum after.

    For a 1D elastic collision, the final velocities are:

    $$v_1' = \frac{(m_1 - m_2) v_1 + 2 m_2 v_2}{m_1 + m_2}$$

    $$v_2' = \frac{(m_2 - m_1) v_2 + 2 m_1 v_1}{m_1 + m_2}$$

    Special case: when the masses are equal ($m_1 = m_2$), the objects simply exchange velocities: $v_1' = v_2$ and $v_2' = v_1$.

    9.7.4 2D Elastic Collision Between Circles

    For 2D collisions between circles, we project the velocities onto the collision normal (the line connecting the centers) and apply the 1D elastic collision formulas to the normal components. The tangential components (perpendicular to the normal) are unchanged. 

    // 2D elastic collision resolution
let normal = p5.Vector.sub(b.pos, a.pos).normalize();
let relVel = p5.Vector.sub(a.vel, b.vel);
let velAlongNormal = relVel.dot(normal);

if (velAlongNormal > 0) return; // Already moving apart

let impulse = (2 * velAlongNormal) / (a.mass + b.mass);
a.vel.sub(p5.Vector.mult(normal, impulse * b.mass));
b.vel.add(p5.Vector.mult(normal, impulse * a.mass));

    9.7.5 Circle-Circle Collision Detection

    Two circles collide when the distance between their centers is less than the sum of their radii:

    $$|\vec{p}_1 - \vec{p}_2| < r_1 + r_2$$

    When a collision is detected, we must also separate the overlapping bodies to prevent them from getting stuck inside each other. We push each body away along the collision normal by half the overlap distance: 

    let overlap = (a.r + b.r) - dist;
let pushVec = normal.copy().mult(overlap * 0.5);
a.pos.sub(pushVec);
b.pos.add(pushVec);
    💡 Key Concept

    Coefficient of restitution ($e$) determines how "bouncy" a collision is. $e = 1$ means perfectly elastic (no energy lost). $e = 0$ means perfectly inelastic (objects stick together). Real-world collisions have $0 < e < 1$. A tennis ball on concrete has $e \approx 0.75$; a lump of clay has $e \approx 0$.

    ✍ Knowledge Check

    In a perfectly elastic collision between two objects of equal mass where one is initially at rest, what happens?

    With equal masses, the elastic collision formulas give $v_1' = v_2$ and $v_2' = v_1$ (the velocities are exchanged). If object 2 was at rest ($v_2 = 0$), then object 1 stops ($v_1' = 0$) and object 2 moves with object 1's original velocity. This is famously demonstrated by Newton's cradle.

    9.8 Coupled Oscillators

    9.8.1 Connected Springs

    A coupled oscillator is a system where multiple oscillators are connected, so the motion of one affects the others. The simplest example is a chain of masses connected by springs. Each mass feels spring forces from its neighbors:

    $$F_i = -k(x_i - x_{i-1}) - k(x_i - x_{i+1}) - b \cdot v_i$$

    where $x_{i-1}$ and $x_{i+1}$ are the positions of the neighboring masses.

    9.8.2 Wave Propagation

    When you disturb one end of a spring chain, the disturbance propagates along the chain as a wave. The speed of the wave depends on the spring constant and the mass: $c = \sqrt{k/m} \cdot d$ where $d$ is the spacing between masses. This is exactly how sound waves propagate through a solid.

    9.8.3 Normal Modes

    A system of $N$ coupled oscillators has $N$ normal modes — special patterns of oscillation where all masses move sinusoidally at the same frequency. The lowest mode has all masses moving in phase; higher modes have more complex patterns with nodes (stationary points). Any motion of the chain can be decomposed into a superposition of normal modes, which is the physical basis of Fourier analysis.

    For a chain of $N$ identical masses connected by identical springs, the normal mode frequencies are:

    $$\omega_k = 2\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \sin\!\left(\frac{k\pi}{2(N+1)}\right), \quad k = 1, 2, \ldots, N$$

    The lowest frequency ($k=1$) is the fundamental mode; higher values of $k$ give harmonics. This is directly analogous to the harmonics of a vibrating string, which is why a guitar string (modeled as a chain of many coupled oscillators) produces a fundamental frequency plus overtones.

    💡 Key Concept

    From particles to waves: A chain of coupled oscillators with many nodes behaves like a continuous wave. The wave equation $\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = c^2 \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}$ emerges as the continuum limit of coupled oscillators when the number of particles approaches infinity. This is the bridge between discrete particle simulations and continuous wave phenomena like sound and light.

    In Hooke's law $F = -kx$, what does the negative sign mean physically?

    The negative sign makes the spring force a restoring force. When the spring is stretched (positive $x$), the force is negative (pulling back). When compressed (negative $x$), the force is positive (pushing out). The force always drives the system toward its rest length.

    9.9 Webcam Effects

    9.9.1 Accessing the Webcam in p5.js

    p5.js can capture live webcam video with a single function call. The createCapture(VIDEO) function creates a video element that streams from the user's camera: 

    let video;

function setup() {
  createCanvas(640, 480);
  video = createCapture(VIDEO);
  video.size(640, 480);
  video.hide(); // Hide the default HTML video element
}

function draw() {
  // Mirror for natural feel
  push();
  translate(width, 0);
  scale(-1, 1);
  image(video, 0, 0);
  pop();
}

    9.9.2 Pixel Manipulation

    The pixel array stores color data as a flat array of RGBA values. For a 640x480 image, that is $640 \times 480 \times 4 = 1{,}228{,}800$ values. Each group of four values represents one pixel: [R, G, B, A, R, G, B, A, ...].

    To convert from (x, y) coordinates to an index in the pixel array:

    $$\text{index} = 4 \times (y \times \text{width} + x)$$

    9.9.3 Common Effects

    Threshold (black and white): Convert each pixel to grayscale and compare to a threshold value.

    let gray = (r + g + b) / 3;
let bw = gray > 128 ? 255 : 0;

    Posterize: Reduce the number of color levels to create a poster-like effect.

    let levels = 4;
let posterized = round(value / 255 * levels) / levels * 255;

    Edge detection (Sobel): Compute the gradient of brightness using neighboring pixels. Strong gradients indicate edges.

    9.9.4 Motion Detection

    Motion detection works by comparing the current frame to the previous frame. Pixels that have changed significantly indicate motion. This is called frame differencing: 

    // For each pixel:
let diff = abs(currentBrightness - previousBrightness);
if (diff > threshold) {
  // Motion detected at this pixel
}

    More sophisticated motion detection uses background subtraction: maintain a slowly-updating average background image, and flag any pixel that differs significantly from this background. This handles gradual lighting changes better than frame differencing.

    9.9.5 Brightness Mirror and Interactive Effects

    A popular creative coding technique is the brightness mirror: replace each pixel with a visual element (circle, character, emoji) whose size or color is based on the pixel's brightness. This transforms the webcam feed into an ASCII art effect or a dynamic mosaic. 

    // Brightness mirror concept
let gridSize = 8;
for (let y = 0; y < video.height; y += gridSize) {
  for (let x = 0; x < video.width; x += gridSize) {
    let idx = 4 * (y * video.width + x);
    let brightness = (video.pixels[idx] + video.pixels[idx+1] + video.pixels[idx+2]) / 3;
    let size = map(brightness, 0, 255, gridSize, 1);
    ellipse(x, y, size, size);
  }
}
    💡 Key Concept

    Performance tip: CPU-based pixel manipulation processes pixels sequentially and can be slow for large images. For real-time performance, either work with a smaller resolution (e.g., 160x120 instead of 640x480) or use GLSL fragment shaders, which process all pixels in parallel on the GPU.

    9.10 Summary

    💡 Chapter Summary
    1. Calculus for simulation: Derivatives measure rates of change (velocity is the derivative of position; acceleration is the derivative of velocity). Integrals accumulate change (position is the integral of velocity). Our simulation loop performs numerical integration frame by frame.
    2. ODEs: Every simulation is an initial value problem for a system of ordinary differential equations. We identify state variables, write rate equations, and integrate numerically.
    3. Integration methods matter: Forward Euler adds energy and is unstable for oscillatory systems. Semi-Implicit (Symplectic) Euler conserves energy approximately and is the recommended method for real-time simulations. Verlet is excellent for constraints. RK4 is the most accurate but not energy-conserving.
    4. Hooke's Law ($F = -kx$) gives us springs, which are the building blocks for ropes, cloth, soft bodies, and coupled oscillators.
    5. Universal Gravitation ($F = Gm_1m_2/r^2$) is an inverse-square law that produces orbits. Always clamp the minimum distance to prevent singularities.
    6. Drag and friction dissipate energy. Friction is constant; quadratic drag grows with speed squared. Terminal velocity occurs when drag balances gravity.
    7. Collisions conserve momentum. Elastic collisions also conserve kinetic energy. For 1D elastic collisions, closed-form velocity formulas exist.
    8. Coupled oscillators produce wave propagation and normal modes, connecting particle physics to wave physics.
    9. Webcam input brings real-world data into simulations. Pixel manipulation enables threshold, posterize, edge detection, and motion detection effects.

    Chapter 9 Review Quiz

    Test your understanding of the physics and simulation concepts covered in this chapter.

    ✍ Question 1

    Which integration method is symplectic (approximately conserves energy)?

    Semi-Implicit (Symplectic) Euler preserves the geometric (symplectic) structure of Hamiltonian systems, which means it approximately conserves energy over long time periods. Forward Euler adds energy, and RK4 is accurate but not symplectic.

    ✍ Question 2

    A spring with $k = 2$ has rest length 50. If the current length is 80, what is the spring force magnitude?

    Displacement $x = 80 - 50 = 30$. Force magnitude $= k \cdot |x| = 2 \times 30 = 60$. The force direction is restoring (toward the rest length).

    ✍ Question 3

    Why do we always clamp the minimum distance in gravitational force calculations?

    Since $F \propto 1/r^2$, as $r$ approaches zero the force approaches infinity, causing extreme velocities that break the simulation. Clamping the minimum distance to a reasonable value prevents this numerical singularity.

    ✍ Question 4

    What is terminal velocity?

    Terminal velocity occurs when drag (which increases with speed) exactly balances gravity (which is constant). At this point, the net force is zero, acceleration is zero, and the object falls at a constant speed: $v_t = \sqrt{mg / c_d}$.

    ✍ Question 5

    In the pixel array pixels[], a 200-pixel-wide image has pixel (x=10, y=5). What is the starting index of this pixel's red channel?

    Index $= 4 \times (y \times \text{width} + x) = 4 \times (5 \times 200 + 10) = 4 \times 1010 = 4040$. Each pixel takes 4 values (R, G, B, A), so we multiply by 4.
