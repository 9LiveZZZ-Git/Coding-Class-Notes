## [Model/modeling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model) ([Conceptual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conceptual_model_(computer_science)))

> ...an informative representation of an object, person or system.
>
> ...can be divided into physical models (e.g. a ship model or a fashion model) and abstract models (e.g. a set of mathematical equations describing the workings of the atmosphere for the purpose of weather forecasting).
>
> ...a model seeks to represent reality with the purpose of better understanding or predicting the world.

We learn a systems parts, rules and relationships, making a simplified conceptual representation of that system. We use this model to simulate a reduced, low-fidelity version of the original system. E.g., a reaction-diffusion _model_. Sometimes we drop model.























## [Physical simulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simulation) ([Examples](https://www.myphysicslab.com))

> ...an imitative representation of a process or system that could exist in the real world. In this broad sense, simulation can often be used interchangeably with model. Sometimes a clear distinction between the two terms is made, in which simulations require the use of models; the model represents the key characteristics or behaviors of the selected system or process, whereas the simulation represents the evolution of the model over time.

To predict the outcomes of a modeled system, given its initial conditions and the laws of physics. We usually visualize and sonify these outcomes; We may allow for interactivity.





















## [Particle system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_system) ([Example](https://editor.p5js.org/p5/sketches/Simulate:_ParticleSystem))

> ...a technique in game physics, motion graphics, and computer graphics that uses many minute sprites, 3D models, or other graphic objects to simulate certain kinds of "fuzzy" phenomena, which are otherwise very hard to reproduce with conventional rendering techniques – usually highly chaotic systems, natural phenomena, or processes caused by chemical reactions.

The behaviour of the tiny elements is governed by laws of physics. We do not try to make these elements intelligent. The particles do not have simulated _agency_.

## [Newton's laws of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion)

1. A body remains at rest, or in motion at a constant speed in a straight line, unless acted upon by a force.
2. When a body is acted upon by a net force, the body's acceleration multiplied by its mass is equal to the net force.
3. If two bodies exert forces on each other, these forces have the same magnitude but opposite directions.

The _second law_ is $F = m a$.

































## [Hook's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hooke%27s_law) ([Example](https://p5js.org/examples/simulate-springs.html))

> ...an empirical law which states that the force (F) needed to extend or compress a spring by some distance (x) scales linearly with respect to that distance—that is, $\vec{F_s} = k_s \vec{x}$, where k is a constant factor characteristic of the spring (i.e., its stiffness), and x is small compared to the total possible deformation of the spring.

```js
function setup() {
  const resting_length = 1.1;
  const a = createVector(1, 1); // spring end point
  const b = createVector(3, 2); // spring end point
  let c = p5.Vector.sub(b, a); // points toward b from a
  print(c.mag());
  const displacement_magnitude = c.mag() - resting_length;
  c.setMag(displacement_magnitude);
  print(c); // force of b on a 
} 
```

## [Coulomb's law ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law) ([Example](https://editor.p5js.org/thechalkface/sketches/8JU0fiB_J))

> ...an experimental law of physics that calculates the amount of force between two electrically charged particles at rest.

$$
F_1 = \frac{q_1 q_2}{4 \pi \epsilon_0 }\frac{\hat r_{12}}{|r_{12}|^2}
$$

```js
function setup() {
  const e0 = 8.8541878128e-12;
  const q1 = 1.1e-10; // charge in coulombs
  const q2 = 7.33-10; // charge in coulombs
  const a = createVector(1, 1); // q1 position
  const b = createVector(3, 2); // q2 position
  const r12 = p5.Vector.sub(b, a); // points to b from a
  const force1 = r12.mult(
    q1 * q2 / (4 * PI * e0 * r12.magSq()));
  print(force1); // force of 2 on 1 
} 
```

## [Newton's law of universal gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_universal_gravitation)

> ...every particle attracts every other particle in the universe with a force that is proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers.

$$
F_{21} = -G \frac{m_1 m_2}{|r_{21}|^2} \hat r_{21}
$$

```js
function setup() {
  const G = 6.6743e-11;
  const m1 = 1.1e7; // mass in kilos
  const m2 = 7.3e7; // mass in kilos
  const a = createVector(1, 1); // m1 position
  const b = createVector(3, 2); // m2 position
  const r21 = p5.Vector.sub(a, b); // points from b to a
  const force21 = r21.mult(G * m1 * m2 / r21.magSq());
  print(force21); // force of 1 on 2 
}
```

## [Friction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friction)

> ...the force resisting the relative motion of solid surfaces, fluid layers, and material elements sliding against each other.
>
> - Dry friction is a force that opposes the relative lateral motion of two solid surfaces in contact.
> - Fluid friction describes the friction between layers of a viscous fluid that are moving relative to each other.
> - Lubricated friction is a case of fluid friction where a lubricant fluid separates two solid surfaces.
> - Skin friction is a component of drag, the force resisting the motion of a fluid across the surface of a body.
> - Internal friction is the force resisting motion between the elements making up a solid material while it undergoes deformation.



Friction is complicated and messy and often nonlinear. There are many friction models for many situations. We may use only one simple kind of friction: [Drag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drag_(physics)).
$$
F_d = c v & \text{or} & F_d = c v^2 \\
$$
Where $c$ is a constant linear factor that represents the amount of drag. We use the $v^2$ form when the *[Reynolds number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reynolds_number)* is high and the $v$ form when it is low.

































## [Derivative/differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative)

> ...shows the sensitivity of change of a function's output with respect to the input.
>
> ...the derivative of the position of a moving object with respect to time is the object's velocity: this measures how quickly the position of the object changes when time advances.

$$
\dot x & \text{Newton's notation} \\
\frac{dx}{dt} & \text{Leibniz' notation} \\
(x_n - x_{n-1})/h & discrete \\ 
\texttt{(current-previous)/timestep} & JavaScript
$$

The instantaneous slope (rise/run) of a function is its derivative, the line tangent to the function at a given point. The derivitive of position is velocity. The derivitive of velocity is acceleration.

































## [Integral/integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral)

> ...the continuous analog of a sum, which is used to calculate areas, volumes, and their generalizations. Integration, the process of computing an integral, is one of the two fundamental operations of calculus, the other being differentiation.

$$
x(t) = \int_0^t{v(t)} & \text{continuous}\\
x[t] = \sum_0^t{v[t]} & \text{discrete} \\
\texttt{position += velocity} & \text{JavaScript}
$$

Integrate acceleration to get velocity. Integrate velocity to get position. It is like the complementary operation to differentiation. The integral of the derivative of a function is the function: $f = \int{\dot{f}}$ .



































## [Euler's method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_method)

$$
y_{n+1} = y_n + h f(t_n, y_n)
$$

...where $f = \dot{y}$ ($f$ is the derivitive of $y$). $h$ is a "time step" chosen by you. This method computes the next likely state of a system given the current state and an expression that computes the derivitive of that state. Or, the next state is probably along the line tangent to the current state. Another way to think about this is... to compute the next state, integrate the derivitive of the state: $f(x) = \int{\dot{f}(x)dx}$. 

- https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-implicit_Euler_method
- https://gafferongames.com/post/integration_basics
- https://gafferongames.com

A _numerical method_ of integration. Drift. Stability.

























## [Ordinary differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation) ([Example](https://editor.p5js.org/kybr/sketches/YLgHJt6Js))

Compartmental model of infectious disease...
$$
\dot{S} = -\beta S I / N & susceptible \\
\dot{I} = -\beta S I / N - \gamma I & infectious \\
\dot{R} = \gamma I & recovered \\
N = S + I + R
$$
The Lorenz system, a meteorological model...
$$
\dot{x} = \sigma (y - x) \\
\dot{y} = x (\rho - z) - y \\
\dot{z} = x y - \beta z \\
$$
Odell's predator-prey model...
$$
\dot{x} = x [ x ( 1 - x ) - y] & \text{rabbits} \\
\dot{y} = y ( x - a ) & \text{wolves} \\
$$

































## [Laws of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laws_of_thermodynamics)

1. In a closed system (i.e. there is no transfer of matter into or out of the system), the first law states that the change in internal energy of the system is equal to the difference between the heat supplied to the system (Q) and the work (W) done by the system on its surroundings.
2. When two initially isolated systems in separate but nearby regions of space, each in thermodynamic equilibrium with itself but not necessarily with each other, are then allowed to interact, they will eventually reach a mutual thermodynamic equilibrium. The sum of the entropies of the initially isolated systems is less than or equal to the total entropy of the final combination.
3. A system's entropy approaches a constant value as its temperature approaches absolute zero.
